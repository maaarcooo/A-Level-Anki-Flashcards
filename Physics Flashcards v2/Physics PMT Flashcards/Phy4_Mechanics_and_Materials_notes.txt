What are scalars? | Physical quantities that describe only a magnitude
What are vectors? | Physical quantities that describe both magnitude and direction
Physical quantities that describe only a magnitude are called? | Scalars
Physical quantities that describe both magnitude and direction are called? | Vectors
Give four examples of scalar quantities. | Distance, speed, mass, temperature
Give four examples of vector quantities. | Displacement, velocity, force/weight, acceleration
When should you use calculation to add vectors? | When the two vectors are perpendicular (at 90° to each other)
When should you use scale drawing to add vectors? | When vectors are at angles other than 90°
What mathematical method is used to find the resultant of two perpendicular vectors? | Pythagoras' theorem
What mathematical method is used to find the direction of a resultant vector? | Trigonometry
What is resolving vectors? | The opposite of adding vectors; splitting a single vector into its perpendicular components using trigonometry
What is the formula for the horizontal component (x) when resolving a vector V at angle θ to the horizontal? | x = V cos θ
What is the formula for the vertical component (y) when resolving a vector V at angle θ to the horizontal? | y = V sin θ
Why is resolving vectors helpful? | Perpendicular vector components don't affect each other, so can be evaluated separately
What condition must be met for an object to be in equilibrium? | The sum of all forces acting on it must be zero
What does an object in equilibrium experience according to Newton's first law? | No resultant force, so it is either at rest or moving at constant velocity
Name two methods to show an object is in equilibrium. | 1) Adding horizontal and vertical components showing they equal zero, 2) Drawing a scale diagram that forms a closed triangle (for 3 forces)
What is the moment of a force? | The force multiplied by the perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the point
What is the formula for moment? | Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance to line of action of force from the point
What is a couple? | A pair of coplanar forces that are equal in magnitude but act in opposite directions
What does coplanar mean? | Forces within the same plane
What is the formula for the moment of a couple? | Moment of a couple = Force × Perpendicular distance between the lines of action of forces
What does the principle of moments state? | For an object in equilibrium, the sum of anticlockwise moments about a pivot is equal to the sum of clockwise moments
What is the centre of mass of an object? | The point at which an object's mass acts
Where is the centre of mass of a uniform object? | Exactly at its centre
What is speed? | A scalar quantity which describes how quickly an object is travelling
What is displacement (s)? | The overall distance travelled from the starting position (includes a direction as it is a vector quantity)
What is velocity (v)? | The rate of change of displacement (Δs/Δt)
What is acceleration (a)? | The rate of change of velocity (Δv/Δt)
What is instantaneous velocity? | The velocity of an object at a specific point in time
How can instantaneous velocity be found from a displacement-time graph? | By drawing a tangent to the graph at the specific time and calculating the gradient
What is average velocity? | The velocity of an object over a specified time frame
How is average velocity calculated? | By dividing the final displacement by the time taken
What is uniform acceleration? | Where the acceleration of an object is constant
What does the area under an acceleration-time graph represent? | Change in velocity
What does the gradient of a velocity-time graph represent? | Acceleration
What does the area under a velocity-time graph represent? | Displacement
What does the gradient of a displacement-time graph represent? | Velocity
State the four SUVAT equations for uniform acceleration. | v = u + at, s = ((u+v)/2)t, s = ut + ½at², v² = u² + 2as
In the SUVAT equations, what does s represent? | Displacement
In the SUVAT equations, what does u represent? | Initial velocity
In the SUVAT equations, what does v represent? | Final velocity
In the SUVAT equations, what does a represent? | Acceleration
In the SUVAT equations, what does t represent? | Time
Why can the vertical and horizontal components of projectile motion be evaluated separately? | They are independent of each other
What is free fall? | Where an object experiences an acceleration of g (9.81 m/s²)
What is friction? | A force which opposes the motion of an object
What are other names for friction when experienced in a fluid? | Drag or air resistance
What do frictional forces convert kinetic energy into? | Other forms such as heat and sound
What happens to the magnitude of air resistance as an object's speed increases? | It increases
What is lift? | An upward force which acts on objects travelling in a fluid, caused by the object creating a change in direction of fluid flow
In which direction does lift act relative to fluid flow? | Perpendicular to the direction of fluid flow
What is terminal speed/terminal velocity? | When frictional forces and driving forces are equal, resulting in no resultant force, no acceleration, and constant speed
Describe how a skydiver reaches terminal velocity. | Initially weight > air resistance causing acceleration; as speed increases, air resistance increases until weight = air resistance, at which point terminal velocity is reached
How does air resistance affect projectile motion? | Maximum height is reached earlier, and both vertical and horizontal distance travelled decrease
State Newton's 1st law. | An object will remain at rest or travelling at a constant velocity until it experiences a resultant force
State Newton's 2nd law. | The acceleration of an object is proportional to the resultant force experienced by the object (F = ma)
State Newton's 3rd law. | For each force experienced by an object, the object exerts an equal and opposite force
What is F = ma? | Newton's second law equation where F is resultant force, m is mass, and a is acceleration
What is a free-body diagram? | A diagram which shows all the forces that act on an object
What is momentum? | The product of mass and velocity of an object
What is the formula for momentum? | Momentum = mass × velocity
When is momentum conserved? | In any interaction where no external forces act
What does conservation of momentum mean for collisions? | Momentum before the collision equals momentum after the collision
What is the relationship between force and momentum (from Newton's 2nd law)? | Force is the rate of change of momentum (F = Δ(mv)/Δt)
What is impulse? | FΔt, which equals the change in momentum
What is the formula relating impulse to change in momentum? | FΔt = Δ(mv)
What does the area under a force-time graph represent? | Change in momentum (impulse)
How do car safety features like crumple zones, seat belts, and air bags reduce injury? | They increase impact time, which decreases the force exerted on passengers
What is an elastic collision? | A collision where both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved
What is an inelastic collision? | A collision where only momentum is conserved, while some kinetic energy is converted into other forms
If objects stick together after a collision, what type of collision is it? | Inelastic collision
Why is an explosion considered an inelastic collision? | Kinetic energy after is greater than before (kinetic energy is not conserved)
What is work done (W)? | The force causing a motion multiplied by the distance travelled in the direction of the force
What is the formula for work done? | W = Fs cos θ (where s is distance travelled and θ is the angle between force and direction of motion)
What is power (P)? | The rate of energy transfer
What is the formula for power in terms of work done? | P = ΔW/Δt
What is the formula for power in terms of force and velocity? | P = Fv
How can work done by a variable force be found? | By calculating the area under a force-displacement graph
What is efficiency? | A measure of how efficiently a system transfers energy
What is the formula for efficiency? | Efficiency = useful output power / input power
How do you convert efficiency to a percentage? | Multiply the efficiency value by 100
What does the principle of conservation of energy state? | Energy cannot be created or destroyed, but can be transferred from one form to another
What happens to total energy in a closed system? | It stays constant (Total energy in = Total energy out)
What is the formula for change in gravitational potential energy? | ΔEp = mgΔh
What is the formula for kinetic energy? | Ek = ½mv²
What is density? | Mass per unit volume; a measure of how compact a substance is
What does Hooke's law state? | Extension is directly proportional to the force applied, given that environmental conditions are kept constant
How is Hooke's law shown on a force-extension graph? | A straight line graph through the origin
What is the limit of proportionality (P)? | The point after which Hooke's law is no longer obeyed
What is the elastic limit (E)? | The point just after the limit of proportionality; beyond this, the material will deform plastically (be permanently stretched)
What is the equation for Hooke's law? | F = kΔL (where k is spring constant and ΔL is extension)
What is the spring constant (k)? | A measure of the stiffness of the spring
What is tensile stress? | Force applied per unit cross-sectional area
What is the formula for tensile stress? | Stress = F/A
What is tensile strain? | Extension divided by original length
What is the formula for tensile strain? | Strain = ΔL/L
What is elastic strain energy? | Energy stored in a material when work is done to stretch or compress it
What is the formula for elastic strain energy? | Elastic strain energy = ½FΔL
How can elastic strain energy be found graphically? | By calculating the area under a force-extension graph
What is breaking stress? | The value of stress at which the material will break apart
What is plastic behaviour on a force-extension graph? | Where a material experiences a large amount of extension as load increases, especially beyond the elastic limit
What is brittle behaviour on a force-extension graph? | Where a material extends very little and is likely to fracture at a low extension
What does the area between loading and unloading lines on a force-extension graph represent? | The work done to permanently deform the material
What happens to energy during elastic deformation? | All work done is stored as elastic strain energy
What happens to energy during plastic deformation? | Work is done to move atoms apart; energy is dissipated as heat rather than stored as elastic strain energy
What do stress-strain graphs describe compared to force-extension graphs? | The behaviour of a material rather than the behaviour of a specific object
What is ultimate tensile stress (UTS)? | The maximum stress a material can withstand; the highest point on a stress-strain graph
What is a ductile material? | A material that can undergo a large amount of plastic deformation before fracturing
What is the Young modulus? | A value which describes the stiffness of a material
What is the formula for Young modulus? | Young modulus (E) = Tensile stress / Tensile strain
What is the alternative formula for Young modulus? | E = FL/(ΔLA)
How can the Young modulus be found from a stress-strain graph? | By finding the gradient of the straight part of the graph
What is the relationship between stress and strain up to the limit of proportionality? | Stress is proportional to strain (their ratio is constant and equals the Young modulus)
What are the units of stress? | Pascals (Pa) or N/m²
What are the units of strain? | No units (dimensionless)
What are the units of the Young modulus? | Pascals (Pa) or N/m²
What are the units of momentum? | kg m/s or N s
What are the units of impulse? | N s or kg m/s
What is the value of g (acceleration due to gravity)? | 9.81 m/s²
