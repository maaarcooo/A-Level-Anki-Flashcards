What is electric current (I)? | The flow of charge per unit time, or the rate of flow of charge
The flow of charge per unit time is the definition of which quantity? | Electric current (I)
What is the formula for electric current? | I = ΔQ/Δt (where Q is charge and t is time)
What is potential difference (V)? | The energy transferred per unit charge between two points in a circuit
The energy transferred per unit charge between two points in a circuit is the definition of which quantity? | Potential difference (V)
What is the formula for potential difference? | V = W/Q (where W is energy transferred and Q is charge)
What is resistance (R)? | A measure of how difficult it is for charge carriers to pass through a component
What is the formula for resistance? | R = V/I
What does Ohm's law state? | For an ohmic conductor, current is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, given that physical conditions (e.g. temperature) are kept constant
What condition must be met for Ohm's law to apply? | Physical conditions (e.g. temperature) must be kept constant
What does the current-voltage graph of an ohmic conductor look like? | A straight line through the origin (provided physical conditions are kept constant)
What is the forward bias of a diode? | The direction in which a diode will allow current to flow easily past the threshold voltage
What is threshold voltage? | The smallest voltage needed to allow current to flow through a diode
What happens to a diode in reverse bias? | The resistance is extremely high, meaning only a very small current can flow
Why does a filament lamp not obey Ohm's law at higher currents? | The metal wire heats up as current increases, causing resistance to increase
At what currents does a filament lamp obey Ohm's law? | At very low currents (when the metal wire does not heat up significantly)
What resistance can ammeters be assumed to have (unless stated otherwise)? | Zero resistance
What resistance can voltmeters be assumed to have (unless stated otherwise)? | Infinite resistance
Why are voltmeters assumed to have infinite resistance? | So that no current flows through them, making their measurement of potential difference exact
What is resistivity (ρ)? | A measure of how easily a material conducts electricity, defined as the product of resistance and cross-sectional area divided by the length of the material
What is the formula for resistivity? | ρ = RA/L (where R is resistance, A is cross-sectional area, L is length)
What does resistivity give the value of? | The resistance through a material of length 1 m and cross-sectional area 1 m²
What factors does resistivity depend on? | Environmental factors such as temperature
What happens to the resistance of a metal conductor when temperature increases? | Resistance increases
Why does the resistance of a metal conductor increase with temperature? | Atoms gain kinetic energy and move more, causing electrons to collide with atoms more frequently, slowing them down and reducing current
What happens to the resistance of a thermistor when temperature increases? | Resistance decreases
Why does the resistance of a thermistor decrease with temperature? | Increasing temperature causes electrons to be emitted from atoms, increasing the number of charge carriers and therefore current
What is one application of a thermistor in circuits? | A temperature sensor that can trigger an event when temperature drops or reaches a certain value
What is a superconductor? | A material which, below a certain temperature (the critical temperature), has zero resistivity
What is the critical temperature of a superconductor? | The temperature below which the superconductor has zero resistivity
Where do most known superconductors have their critical temperatures? | Close to 0 K (-273°C)
What happens to resistance when resistivity drops to zero in a superconductor? | Resistance also drops to zero
Name two applications of superconductors | Power cables (reducing energy loss to zero) and strong magnetic fields (for maglev trains and medical applications)
Why are superconductors useful in power cables? | They would reduce energy loss through heating to zero during transmission
Why are superconductors useful for creating strong magnetic fields? | They would not require a constant power source
What is the formula for total resistance of resistors in series? | R_T = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...
What is the formula for total resistance of resistors in parallel? | 1/R_T = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...
What is the formula for power in terms of energy and time? | P = E/t (where E is energy transferred and t is time)
What is power (P)? | The energy transferred over time (rate of transfer of energy)
The rate of transfer of energy is the definition of which quantity? | Power (P)
What is the formula for power in terms of voltage and current? | P = VI
What is the formula for power in terms of voltage and resistance? | P = V²/R
What is the formula for power in terms of current and resistance? | P = I²R
What is the formula for energy transferred in terms of voltage, current and time? | E = VIt
In a series circuit, what is true about the current? | The current is the same everywhere in the circuit
In a series circuit, what is true about the voltages? | The total sum of the voltages across all elements equals the supply p.d.
In a parallel circuit, what is true about the currents? | The sum of the currents in each parallel set of branches equals the total current
In a parallel circuit, what is true about the potential difference? | The potential difference across each branch is the same
When identical cells are joined in series, what is the total voltage? | The sum of the individual voltages of the cells (V_T = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ...)
When identical cells are joined in parallel, what is the total voltage? | Equal to the voltage of one cell (V_T = V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = ...)
What two quantities are always conserved in DC circuits? | Charge and energy
What does Kirchhoff's first law state? | The total current flowing into a junction equals the current flowing out of that junction
What does Kirchhoff's first law show about charge? | No charge is lost at any point in the circuit
What does Kirchhoff's second law state? | The sum of all the voltages in a series circuit equals the battery voltage
What does Kirchhoff's second law show about energy? | No energy is lost at any point in a circuit
What is a potential divider? | A circuit with several resistors in series connected across a voltage source, used to produce a required fraction of the source potential difference
How can a potential divider supply a variable potential difference? | By using a variable resistor as one of the resistors in series
In a potential divider, what happens to output p.d. if resistance across R₁ increases? | Output p.d. decreases (because circuit current has decreased and V = IR)
What happens to a light dependent resistor's (LDR) resistance as light intensity increases? | Resistance decreases
What causes internal resistance in a battery? | Electrons colliding with atoms inside the battery, causing energy loss before electrons leave the battery
What is electromotive force (emf/ε)? | The energy transferred by a cell per coulomb of charge that passes through it
The energy transferred by a cell per coulomb of charge is the definition of which quantity? | Electromotive force (emf/ε)
What is the formula for emf in terms of energy and charge? | ε = E/Q
What is the relationship between total resistance, internal resistance and load resistance? | R_T = R + r (total = load + internal)
What is the formula for emf in terms of current and resistances? | ε = IR + Ir or ε = I(R + r)
What is terminal p.d. (V)? | The p.d. across the load resistor R in a circuit with internal resistance
What are lost volts (v)? | The p.d. across the internal resistance r, equal to the energy wasted by the cell per coulomb of charge
What is the formula relating emf, terminal p.d. and lost volts? | ε = V + v
What is the formula for terminal p.d.? | V = IR
What is the formula for lost volts? | v = Ir
How can the emf of a battery be measured? | By measuring the voltage across a cell using a voltmeter when there is no current running through the cell (open circuit)
Why must there be no current flowing when measuring emf with a voltmeter? | So that there are no lost volts (v = Ir = 0 when I = 0), meaning the voltmeter reads the true emf
