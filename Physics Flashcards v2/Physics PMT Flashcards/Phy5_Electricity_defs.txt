What is an ammeter? | A device that measures the current in the loop of the circuit that it is connected in series with
What resistance does an ideal ammeter have? | Zero resistance
How must an ammeter be connected in a circuit? | In series
What device measures current and is connected in series? | Ammeter
What is current? | The rate of flow of charge in a circuit
What is the rate of flow of charge in a circuit called? | Current
What is electromotive force (emf)? | The amount of energy transferred by a source to each unit of charge that passes through it
What quantity describes the energy transferred by a source per unit charge? | Electromotive force (emf)
What is internal resistance? | The resistance to the flow of charge within a source
What causes energy to be dissipated within a power source? | Internal resistance
What is the resistance to flow of charge within a source called? | Internal resistance
What is a light dependent resistor (LDR)? | A light sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases when light intensity decreases
How does an LDR's resistance change when light intensity decreases? | It increases
What type of component has resistance that increases when light intensity decreases? | Light dependent resistor (LDR)
What is an ohmic conductor? | A conductor for which current flow is directly proportional to potential difference across it, when under constant physical conditions
What condition must be maintained for a conductor to be ohmic? | Constant physical conditions
What is Ohm's Law? | Current and potential difference through an ohmic conductor held under constant physical conditions are directly proportional, with the constant of proportionality being resistance
What is the constant of proportionality in Ohm's Law? | Resistance
When are components connected in parallel? | When they are connected across each other (separate loops)
What is the arrangement called when components are connected across each other in separate loops? | Parallel circuits
What is a potential divider? | A method of splitting a potential difference by connecting two resistors in series
How is the total potential difference split in a potential divider? | In the ratio of the resistances
What is resistance? | A measure of how difficult it is for current to flow through a material
What quantity measures how difficult it is for current to flow through a material? | Resistance
What is resistivity? | A quantity proportional to an object's resistance and cross-sectional area, and inversely proportional to the object's length
What is resistivity proportional to? | Resistance and cross-sectional area
What is resistivity inversely proportional to? | Length
What is the same across all resistors connected in parallel? | Potential difference
What happens to current in a parallel circuit? | It is split between the resistors
How do you calculate total resistance for resistors in parallel? | The inverse of the sum of the inverses of the individual resistances
What is the same through all resistors connected in series? | Current
How is potential difference distributed across resistors in series? | Split in the ratio of their resistances
How do you calculate total resistance for resistors in series? | The sum of the resistances of the resistors
When are components connected in series? | When they are connected end to end (in one loop)
What is the arrangement called when components are connected end to end in one loop? | Series circuits
What is a superconductor? | A material which has zero resistivity when temperature is decreased to or below the material's critical temperature
What resistivity does a superconductor have below its critical temperature? | Zero resistivity
What are two applications of superconductors? | Producing strong magnetic fields and reducing energy loss when transmitting electric power
What is the critical temperature of a superconductor? | The temperature at or below which the material has zero resistivity
What is terminal potential difference? | The potential difference across the terminals of a power source
How is terminal potential difference related to emf and internal resistance? | It equals the source's emf minus any voltage drop over the source's internal resistance
What is a thermistor? | A temperature sensitive semiconductor whose resistance increases when temperature decreases
How does a thermistor's resistance change when temperature decreases? | It increases
What type of component has resistance that increases when temperature decreases? | Thermistor
What is a voltmeter? | A device used to measure the potential difference across components
What resistance does an ideal voltmeter have? | Infinite resistance
How must a voltmeter be connected to measure potential difference? | In parallel across the component
What device measures potential difference and is connected in parallel? | Voltmeter
