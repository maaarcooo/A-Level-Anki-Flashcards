What is amplitude? | A wave's maximum displacement from its equilibrium position.
A wave's maximum displacement from its equilibrium position is called what? | Amplitude
What is an antinode? | A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave.
A position of maximum displacement in a stationary wave is called what? | An antinode
What is cladding? | A protective layer on an optical fibre to improve tensile strength, prevent scratching, and prevent signal transfer between adjacent fibres.
What are the three functions of cladding on an optical fibre? | Improve tensile strength, prevent scratching, and prevent signal transfer between adjacent fibres.
What is coherence? | Waves are coherent if they have the same wavelength and frequency, and a fixed phase difference between them.
What three conditions must be met for waves to be coherent? | Same wavelength, same frequency, and a fixed phase difference.
What is a diffraction grating? | A grating with hundreds of slits per millimetre that produces sharper interference patterns.
What are diffraction gratings used for? | To calculate atomic spacing and to analyse elements.
What is diffraction? | The spreading of waves as they pass through a gap of similar magnitude to their wavelength.
When does significant diffraction occur? | When waves pass through a gap of similar magnitude to their wavelength.
What are electromagnetic waves? | Waves that consist of perpendicular electric and magnetic oscillations.
What is frequency? | The number of waves that pass a point in a unit time period; the inverse of the time period.
How is frequency related to time period? | Frequency is the inverse of the time period.
What is fringe spacing? | The distance between two adjacent bright fringes or two adjacent dark fringes.
What is interference? | The superposition of waves that occurs when two waves meet.
What happens when two waves in phase interfere? | They constructively interfere.
What happens when two waves out of phase interfere? | They destructively interfere.
What is a laser? | A light source that produces a collimated and coherent beam.
What two properties describe the beam produced by a laser? | Collimated and coherent.
What is a longitudinal wave? | A wave with oscillations parallel to the direction of energy propagation.
Give an example of a longitudinal wave. | Sound waves.
What is material dispersion? | Waves of different wavelengths travel at slightly different speeds through an optical fibre, reaching the end at different times, causing pulse broadening.
How can material dispersion be reduced? | By using monochromatic light.
What is modal dispersion? | Waves entering an optical fibre at slightly different angles travel different distances, reaching the end at different times, causing pulse broadening.
What is a node? | A position of minimum displacement in a stationary wave.
A position of minimum displacement in a stationary wave is called what? | A node
What is an optical fibre? | A thin glass fibre through which signals are passed, usually surrounded by cladding.
What is path difference? | A measure of how far ahead a wave is compared to another wave, usually expressed in terms of wavelength.
In what units is path difference usually expressed? | In terms of wavelength.
What is phase difference? | The difference in phase between two points on a wave, usually expressed in radians.
In what units is phase difference usually expressed? | Radians.
What is phase? | A measure of how far through the wave's cycle a given point on the wave is.
What is polarisation? | The restriction of a wave so that it can only oscillate in a single plane.
Which type of wave can be polarised? | Transverse waves only.
What is pulse broadening? | The elongation of a signal passed down an optical fibre.
What are the two main causes of pulse broadening? | Modal dispersion and material dispersion.
What is refractive index? | A material property equal to the ratio between the speed of light in a vacuum and the speed of light in a given material.
What is Snell's Law? | A law linking a wave's angle of incidence to its angle of refraction using the refractive indexes of the mediums involved.
How is wave speed calculated? | Speed = frequency Ã— wavelength.
What is a stationary wave? | A wave that stores, but does not transfer, energy.
What is total internal reflection? | Full reflection at the inside boundary of an optical fibre, where no radiation passes out.
Where does total internal reflection occur? | In optical fibres, at the inside boundary of the fibre.
What is a transverse wave? | A wave with oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy propagation.
Give an example of a transverse wave. | Electromagnetic waves.
What is wavelength? | The distance between two identical positions on two adjacent waves.
How is wavelength commonly measured? | From peak to peak or trough to trough.
What is Young's double-slit experiment? | An experiment demonstrating diffraction of light by passing monochromatic light across two narrow slits and observing the resulting pattern of bright and dark fringes.
What type of light is used in Young's double-slit experiment? | Monochromatic light.
What pattern is observed in Young's double-slit experiment? | A pattern of bright and dark fringes.
