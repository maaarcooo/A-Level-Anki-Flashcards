What is breaking stress? | The maximum stress that an object can withstand before failure occurs.
The maximum stress an object can withstand before failure occurs is called what? | Breaking stress
What does it mean for a material to be brittle? | A brittle object will show very little strain before reaching its breaking stress.
A material that shows very little strain before reaching its breaking stress is described as what? | Brittle
What is the centre of mass? | The single point through which all the mass of an object can be said to act.
The single point through which all the mass of an object can be said to act is called what? | Centre of mass
State the principle of conservation of energy. | Energy cannot be created or destroyed - it can only be transferred into different forms.
What principle states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred into different forms? | Conservation of energy
State the principle of conservation of momentum. | The total momentum of a system before an event must be equal to the total momentum of the system after the event, assuming no external forces act.
What condition must be met for momentum to be conserved? | No external forces act on the system.
What is a couple? | Two equal and opposite parallel forces that act on an object through different lines of action.
What effect does a couple have on an object? | It causes a rotation without translation.
Two equal and opposite parallel forces acting through different lines of action is called what? | A couple
What is density? | The mass per unit volume of a material.
Mass per unit volume of a material is the definition of what? | Density
What is efficiency? | The ratio of useful output to total input for a given system.
The ratio of useful output to total input is the definition of what? | Efficiency
What is elastic behaviour? | When a material deforms elastically, it will return to its original shape when the deforming forces are removed.
What happens to an object showing elastic behaviour when the deforming forces are removed? | It returns to its original shape and is not permanently deformed.
What is an elastic collision? | A collision in which the total kinetic energy of the system before the collision is equal to the total kinetic energy of the system after the collision.
In what type of collision is kinetic energy conserved? | An elastic collision
What is the elastic limit? | The force beyond which an object will no longer deform elastically, and instead deform plastically.
What happens to an object stretched beyond its elastic limit? | It will not return to its original shape when the deforming forces are removed.
The force beyond which an object deforms plastically instead of elastically is called what? | The elastic limit
What is elastic strain energy? | The energy stored in an object when it is stretched.
How is elastic strain energy related to work done? | Elastic strain energy is equal to the work done to stretch the object.
How can elastic strain energy be determined from a graph? | From the area under a force-extension graph.
The area under a force-extension graph represents what? | Elastic strain energy (or work done to stretch the object)
What are the two conditions for an object to be in equilibrium? | The resultant force and resultant moment acting on the object must both be equal to zero.
For an object to be in equilibrium, the resultant force must equal what? | Zero
For an object to be in equilibrium, the resultant moment must equal what? | Zero
State Hooke's Law. | The extension of an elastic object will be directly proportional to the force applied to it up to the object's limit of proportionality.
What does Hooke's Law describe? | The relationship between extension and applied force for an elastic object up to the limit of proportionality.
What is impulse? | The change of momentum of an object when a force acts on it.
How is impulse calculated? | The product of the force acting on the object and the length of time over which it acts.
The product of force and time is equal to what? | Impulse (or change in momentum)
What is an inelastic collision? | A collision in which the total kinetic energy of the system before the collision is not equal to the kinetic energy of the system after the collision.
In what type of collision is kinetic energy not conserved? | An inelastic collision
What is a moment? | The product of a force and the perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot.
The product of force and perpendicular distance to the pivot is called what? | A moment
What is momentum? | The product of an object's mass and velocity.
The product of mass and velocity is called what? | Momentum
State Newton's First Law. | An object will remain in its current state of motion, unless acted on by a resultant force.
What is required for an object to accelerate? | A resultant force
What does Newton's First Law state about an object with no resultant force? | It will remain in its current state of motion.
State Newton's Second Law. | The sum of the forces acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of momentum of the object.
What is equal to the rate of change of momentum? | The sum of the forces acting on an object (resultant force)
State Newton's Third Law. | Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
According to Newton's Third Law, if object A exerts a force on object B, what must happen? | Object B must exert a force back on object A that is opposite in direction and equal in magnitude.
What is plastic behaviour? | When a material deforms plastically, it will not return to its original shape when the deforming forces are removed.
What happens to an object showing plastic behaviour when the deforming forces are removed? | It will be permanently deformed and will not return to its original shape.
State the Principle of Moments. | For an object to be in equilibrium, the sum of the clockwise moments acting about a point must be equal to the sum of the anticlockwise moments acting about the point.
For equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments must equal what? | The sum of anticlockwise moments about the same point.
What is a scalar quantity? | A quantity that only has a magnitude.
Give three examples of scalar quantities. | Length, mass, and temperature.
A quantity that only has magnitude (no direction) is called what? | A scalar
What is the spring constant? | The constant of proportionality for the extension of a spring under a force.
What does a higher spring constant indicate? | The greater the force needed to achieve a given extension.
The constant of proportionality between force and extension for a spring is called what? | Spring constant
What is stiffness? | A measure of how difficult it is to stretch a given object.
A measure of how difficult it is to stretch an object is called what? | Stiffness
What is tensile strain? | The ratio of an object's extension to its original length.
Why does tensile strain have no unit? | Because it is a ratio of two lengths.
The ratio of extension to original length is called what? | Tensile strain
What is tensile stress? | The amount of force acting per unit area.
What is the unit of tensile stress? | The Pascal (Pa)
Force per unit area is the definition of what? | Tensile stress
What is terminal speed? | The maximum speed of an object that occurs when the resistive and driving forces acting on the object are equal to each other.
At terminal speed, what is the relationship between resistive and driving forces? | They are equal to each other.
When resistive forces equal driving forces, what speed has the object reached? | Terminal speed
What is a vector quantity? | A quantity that has both a magnitude and a direction.
Give three examples of vector quantities. | Velocity, displacement, and acceleration.
A quantity that has both magnitude and direction is called what? | A vector
What is the Young modulus? | The ratio of stress to strain for a given material.
What is the unit of Young modulus? | The Pascal (Pa)
The ratio of stress to strain is called what? | Young modulus
What quantity is conserved in an elastic collision? | Total kinetic energy (as well as momentum)
What quantity is always conserved in any collision (elastic or inelastic)? | Momentum (assuming no external forces)
What is the difference between elastic and plastic deformation? | Elastic deformation is reversible (object returns to original shape), plastic deformation is permanent.
What is the limit of proportionality? | The point up to which extension is directly proportional to applied force (Hooke's Law applies).
What happens to a spring stretched beyond the limit of proportionality? | Extension is no longer directly proportional to force (Hooke's Law no longer applies).
