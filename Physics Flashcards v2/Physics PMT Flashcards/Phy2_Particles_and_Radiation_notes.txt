What are the three constituents of an atom? | Protons, neutrons and electrons
What are nucleons? | Protons and neutrons (particles found in the nucleus)
What is the charge of a proton in coulombs? | +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the relative charge of a proton? | +1
What is the mass of a proton in kg? | 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the relative mass of a proton? | 1
What is the specific charge of a proton? | 9.58 × 10⁷ C kg⁻¹
What is the charge of a neutron? | 0
What is the mass of a neutron in kg? | 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the relative mass of a neutron? | 1
What is the charge of an electron in coulombs? | -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the relative charge of an electron? | -1
What is the mass of an electron in kg? | 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg
What is the relative mass of an electron? | 0.0005
What is the specific charge of an electron? | 1.76 × 10¹¹ C kg⁻¹
What is specific charge? | The charge-mass ratio of a particle, calculated by dividing charge by mass
How do you calculate specific charge? | Specific charge = Charge / Mass
What does the proton number (Z) represent? | The number of protons in an atom
What does the nucleon number (A) represent? | The number of protons and neutrons in an atom
What are isotopes? | Atoms with the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
What is carbon dating used for? | Finding the approximate age of an object containing organic material
What does the strong nuclear force (SNF) do? | Keeps nuclei stable by counteracting the electrostatic force of repulsion between protons
What particles does the strong nuclear force act on? | Nucleons only
At what separation is the strong nuclear force attractive? | Up to separations of 3 fm
At what separation is the strong nuclear force repulsive? | Below separations of 0.5 fm
What are unstable nuclei? | Nuclei with too many protons, neutrons or both, causing the SNF to not be enough to keep them stable
When does alpha decay occur? | In large nuclei with too many of both protons and neutrons
How does the proton number change in alpha decay? | Decreases by 2
How does the nucleon number change in alpha decay? | Decreases by 4
When does beta-minus decay occur? | In nuclei which are neutron-rich (have too many neutrons)
How does the proton number change in beta-minus decay? | Increases by 1
How does the nucleon number change in beta-minus decay? | Stays the same
Why were neutrinos hypothesised? | To account for energy not being conserved in beta-minus decay observations
What is an antiparticle? | A particle with the same rest energy and mass as its corresponding particle, but with opposite values for all other properties
What is the antiparticle of an electron? | Positron
What is the rest energy of an electron in MeV? | 0.511 MeV
What is the rest energy of a positron in MeV? | 0.511 MeV
What is the charge of a positron? | +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the rest energy of an electron neutrino? | 0
What is the charge of an electron neutrino? | 0
What are photons? | Packets of electromagnetic radiation that transfer energy and have no mass
What is the equation for photon energy? | E = hf = hc/λ
What is the value of Planck's constant? | 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J s
What is annihilation? | When a particle and its corresponding antiparticle collide, converting their masses into energy released as 2 photons moving in opposite directions
Why do the two photons from annihilation move in opposite directions? | To conserve momentum
How does a PET scanner work? | A positron-emitting radioisotope is introduced into the patient; positrons annihilate with electrons, emitting detectable gamma photons
What is pair production? | When a photon is converted into an equal amount of matter and antimatter
What condition must be met for pair production to occur? | The photon must have energy greater than the total rest energy of both particles produced
What happens to excess energy in pair production? | It is converted into kinetic energy of the particles
What are the four fundamental forces? | Gravity, electromagnetic, weak nuclear and strong nuclear
What are exchange particles? | Particles that carry energy and momentum between particles experiencing a force
What is the exchange particle for the strong interaction? | Gluon
What is the range of the strong interaction? | 3 × 10⁻¹⁵ m
What particles does the strong interaction act on? | Hadrons
What are the exchange particles for the weak interaction? | W bosons (W⁺ or W⁻)
What is the range of the weak interaction? | 10⁻¹⁸ m
What particles does the weak interaction act on? | All particles
What is the exchange particle for the electromagnetic interaction? | Virtual photon (γ)
What is the range of the electromagnetic interaction? | Infinite
What particles does the electromagnetic interaction act on? | Charged particles
What interactions is the weak nuclear force responsible for? | Beta decay, electron capture, and electron-proton collisions
What is the equation for electron capture? | p + e⁻ → n + νₑ
What is the equation for beta-plus decay? | p → n + e⁺ + νₑ
What is the equation for beta-minus decay? | n → p + e⁻ + ν̄ₑ
What are hadrons? | Particles formed of quarks that experience the strong nuclear force
What are leptons? | Fundamental particles that do not experience the strong nuclear force
What are baryons? | Hadrons formed of 3 quarks
What are antibaryons? | Hadrons formed of 3 antiquarks
What are mesons? | Hadrons formed from a quark and an antiquark
What does baryon number indicate? | Whether a particle is a baryon (+1), antibaryon (-1) or not a baryon (0)
Is baryon number conserved in particle interactions? | Yes, always
Which is the only stable baryon? | The proton
What happens to all baryons eventually? | They decay into protons either directly or indirectly
What does lepton number indicate? | Whether a particle is a lepton (+1), antilepton (-1) or not a lepton (0)
What are the two types of lepton number you need to know? | Electron lepton number and muon lepton number
Is lepton number conserved in particle interactions? | Yes, always
What is a muon sometimes known as? | A "heavy electron"
What do muons decay into? | Electrons
What are strange particles? | Particles produced by the strong nuclear interaction but decay by the weak interaction
What are kaons? | Strange particles that decay into pions through the weak interaction
What is strangeness? | A property of particles that shows strange particles must be created in pairs
Is strangeness conserved in strong interactions? | Yes
How can strangeness change in weak interactions? | By 0, +1 or -1
Why do particle physics investigations rely on international collaboration? | Particle accelerators are very expensive to build and run, and produce huge amounts of data
What are quarks? | Fundamental particles which make up hadrons
What is the charge of an up quark? | +⅔e
What is the baryon number of an up quark? | +⅓
What is the strangeness of an up quark? | 0
What is the charge of a down quark? | -⅓e
What is the baryon number of a down quark? | +⅓
What is the strangeness of a down quark? | 0
What is the charge of a strange quark? | -⅓e
What is the baryon number of a strange quark? | +⅓
What is the strangeness of a strange quark? | -1
How do the properties of antiquarks compare to quarks? | They have the opposite sign
What is the quark composition of a proton? | uud
What is the quark composition of a neutron? | udd
What is the quark composition of π⁰? | uū or dd̄
What is the quark composition of π⁺? | ud̄
What is the quark composition of π⁻? | ūd
What is the quark composition of K⁺? | us̄
What is the quark composition of K⁻? | ūs
What is the quark composition of K⁰? | ds̄ or d̄s
What properties must always be conserved in particle interactions? | Energy and momentum, charge, baryon number, electron lepton number, muon lepton number
When must strangeness be conserved? | Only during strong interactions
What quark change occurs in beta-minus decay? | A down quark changes into an up quark
What quark change occurs in beta-plus decay? | An up quark changes into a down quark
Why are beta decays caused by the weak interaction? | Because there is a change of quark type
What is the photoelectric effect? | The emission of photoelectrons from the surface of a metal after light above a certain frequency is shone on it
What is the threshold frequency? | The minimum frequency of light required to cause photoelectric emission from a metal
Why couldn't wave theory explain the threshold frequency? | Wave theory suggests any frequency should cause emission as energy would gradually increase with each wave
How does the photon model explain the threshold frequency? | Each electron absorbs a single photon, so emission only occurs if photon energy (determined by frequency) is sufficient
What happens when light intensity is increased above threshold frequency? | More photoelectrons are emitted per second
What is the work function of a metal? | The minimum energy required for electrons to be emitted from the surface of a metal (symbol: φ)
What is stopping potential? | The potential difference needed to stop the photoelectrons with maximum kinetic energy
How is maximum kinetic energy related to stopping potential? | Eₖ(max) = eVₛ
What is the photoelectric equation? | E = hf = φ + Eₖ(max)
Can electrons in atoms exist at any energy level? | No, they can only exist in discrete energy levels
What is excitation? | When electrons gain energy from collisions with free electrons and move up to a higher energy level
What is ionisation? | When an electron gains enough energy to be removed from the atom entirely
When does ionisation occur? | When the energy of the free electron is greater than the ionisation energy
What happens when an excited electron returns to ground state? | It releases the energy it gained in the form of a photon
What gas fills a fluorescent tube? | Mercury vapour
What does the fluorescent coating on a tube do? | Absorbs UV photons and emits visible light photons when electrons de-excite
What is an electron volt (eV)? | The energy gained by one electron when passing through a potential difference of 1 volt
How many joules is 1 eV? | 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
How do you convert from eV to joules? | Multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
How do you convert from joules to eV? | Divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
What is a line spectrum? | A spectrum containing only discrete values of wavelength (not continuous)
What does a line spectrum demonstrate about electrons? | Electrons in atoms can only transition between discrete energy levels
What is a line absorption spectrum? | A continuous spectrum with black lines at certain wavelengths
What do the black lines in an absorption spectrum represent? | Wavelengths corresponding to photon energies equal to differences between energy levels
What is the equation relating photon energy to energy level transitions? | hf = E₁ - E₂
What evidence shows light has wave properties? | Diffraction and interference
What evidence shows light has particle properties? | The photoelectric effect
What evidence shows electrons have wave properties? | Electron diffraction
What did de Broglie hypothesise? | If light has particle properties, then particles should also have wave-like properties
What is the de Broglie wavelength equation? | λ = h/mv
What happens to diffraction pattern when particle momentum increases? | Wavelength decreases, so concentric rings become closer together
What happens to diffraction pattern when particle momentum decreases? | Wavelength increases, so concentric rings move further apart
What is wave-particle duality? | The phenomenon where light and matter exhibit both wave and particle properties
What is peer review? | The process by which experimental evidence is validated by the scientific community before being accepted
