What are stationary waves (standing waves) produced by? | The superposition of two waves of the same frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions
How is a stationary wave usually achieved in practice? | By a travelling wave and its reflection
What is a stationary wave? | A wave pattern where the peaks and troughs do not move
What do stationary waves do with energy? | Store energy
What do progressive waves do with energy? | Transfer energy
What is a node? | A point of no vibration (zero displacement)
A point of no vibration (zero displacement) is called what? | A node
What is an antinode? | A point of maximum amplitude
A point of maximum amplitude is called what? | An antinode
Do nodes and antinodes move along a string in a stationary wave? | No, nodes are fixed and antinodes only move in the vertical direction
What are the only two phase relationships between points on a stationary wave? | In phase or out of phase (antiphase)
What is the phase relationship between points located between adjacent nodes? | They are in phase with each other
What is the phase relationship between points separated by an odd number of nodes? | They are out of phase (antiphase)
What is the phase relationship between points separated by an even number of nodes? | They are in phase
In progressive waves, what is true about the amplitude of all points? | All points have the same amplitude (in turn)
In stationary waves, what is true about the amplitude of each point? | Each point has a different amplitude depending on the amount of superposition
What is the phase of points exactly one wavelength apart in a progressive wave? | In phase
What is the range of phase difference between points within one wavelength in a progressive wave? | Between 0° and 360°
What is the phase relationship between points on either side of a node in a stationary wave? | Out of phase (antiphase)
Do progressive waves have nodes and antinodes? | No
Do stationary waves have nodes and antinodes? | Yes
What does wave speed represent in a progressive wave? | The speed at which the wave moves through a medium
What does wave speed represent in a stationary wave? | The speed at which each point oscillates (the overall wave does not move)
What is the distance between two adjacent nodes in a stationary wave? | Half a wavelength (λ/2)
What is the distance between two adjacent antinodes in a stationary wave? | Half a wavelength (λ/2)
What is the distance between a node and an adjacent antinode? | Quarter of a wavelength (λ/4)
What does the principle of superposition state? | When two or more waves with the same frequency arrive at a point, the resultant displacement is the sum of the displacements of each wave
When two waves with the same frequency and amplitude arrive at a point in phase, what occurs? | Constructive interference
When two waves with the same frequency and amplitude arrive at a point in antiphase, what occurs? | Destructive interference
What happens to amplitude during constructive interference of two identical waves? | The resultant wave has double the amplitude
What happens to amplitude during destructive interference of two identical waves? | The resultant wave has no amplitude (zero)
Does the principle of superposition apply to longitudinal waves? | Yes, it applies to all types of waves (transverse and longitudinal, progressive and stationary)
What three conditions must be met for a stationary wave to form? | Two waves travelling in opposite directions along the same line, with the same frequency (and wavelength), and similar amplitude
What causes nodes in a stationary wave? | Destructive interference where waves are in antiphase
What causes antinodes in a stationary wave? | Constructive interference where waves are in phase
What produces vibrations that create sound in stringed instruments? | Stationary waves on a stretched string
What are resonant frequencies? | Specific frequencies at which a whole number of half wavelengths fit on the length of a string, forming stationary waves
In the microwave stationary wave demonstration, what is used to detect nodes and antinodes? | A small probe detector moved between the microwave source and reflecting plate
How can stationary sound waves be demonstrated in an air column? | By placing fine powder inside an air column with a loudspeaker at the open end
What happens to powder in a tube when stationary sound waves form? | The powder forms evenly spaced heaps at the nodes (zero disturbance points)
For a stationary wave in an air column, what must be present at each end? | A node at the closed end and an antinode at the open end (loudspeaker end)
What is another name for the first harmonic? | The fundamental frequency
What is another name for the fundamental frequency? | The first harmonic
Describe the first harmonic pattern on a string fixed at both ends. | A single loop with two nodes (at each end) and one antinode (in the middle)
What is the wavelength of the first harmonic for a string of length L? | λ = 2L
What is the frequency equation for the first harmonic? | f₁ = v/(2L)
How many nodes does the second harmonic have? | Three nodes
How many antinodes does the second harmonic have? | Two antinodes
What is the wavelength of the second harmonic for a string of length L? | λ = L
What is the frequency of the second harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f₂ = 2f₁
How many nodes does the third harmonic have? | Four nodes
How many antinodes does the third harmonic have? | Three antinodes
What is the wavelength of the third harmonic for a string of length L? | λ = 2L/3
What is the frequency of the third harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | f₃ = 3f₁
For the nth harmonic, how many antinodes are there? | n antinodes
For the nth harmonic, how many nodes are there? | n + 1 nodes
What is the frequency of the nth harmonic in terms of the first harmonic? | fₙ = n × f₁
What is the equation for wave speed on a string under tension? | v = √(T/μ)
In the equation v = √(T/μ), what does T represent? | Tension in the string (N)
In the equation v = √(T/μ), what does μ represent? | Mass per unit length of the string (kg m⁻¹)
What is the equation for the frequency of the first harmonic on a string? | f = (1/2L)√(T/μ)
How do you calculate mass per unit length (μ)? | μ = mass of string / length of string
What three variables can be investigated in the stationary waves required practical? | Length of string, tension in string, and mass per unit length (different strings)
In the stationary waves practical, what is the purpose of the signal generator? | To operate the vibration generator and measure the frequency of the first harmonic
In the stationary waves practical, what is the purpose of the vibration generator? | To produce the stationary wave on the string
In the stationary waves practical, what provides the tension in the string? | Masses hanging from a pulley (T = mg)
In the stationary waves practical, what is the purpose of the bridge? | To provide a fixed end and allow variation of the string length
How is the first harmonic identified in the stationary waves practical? | When the largest vibration/amplitude is observed on the string
In the stationary waves practical, how is tension calculated? | T = mg, where m is the hanging mass and g = 9.81 N kg⁻¹
For the f vs 1/L graph in the stationary waves practical, what does the gradient equal? | v/2 (half the wave speed)
How is wave speed calculated from the f vs 1/L graph? | v = 2 × gradient
What systematic error can be reduced by using an oscilloscope in the stationary waves practical? | Verifying the signal generator's frequency readings
How long should the signal generator be left before taking measurements? | About 20 minutes to stabilise
What is the main source of random error in the stationary waves practical? | The sharpness of resonance makes it difficult to decide exactly when the first harmonic is achieved
What technique helps identify the first harmonic more accurately? | Adjusting the frequency while looking closely at a node to find the largest response
Why is looking at amplitude less reliable for identifying the first harmonic? | Because the wave will be moving very fast
What safety precaution should be taken if using a metal wire? | Wear goggles to protect eyes in case the wire snaps under tension
Why might a rubber string be preferred over a metal wire? | It is less dangerous if it snaps under tension
What safety precaution relates to the hanging masses? | Stand well away and place a crash mat underneath in case they fall

When combining percentage uncertainties for quantities raised to a power of ½, what happens to the uncertainty? | The percentage uncertainty is halved
