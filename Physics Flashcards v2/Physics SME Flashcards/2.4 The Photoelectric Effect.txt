What is the electronvolt commonly used to express? | Very small energies (quantum energies much smaller than 1 Joule)
What is the definition of an electronvolt? | The energy gained by an electron travelling through a potential difference of one volt
What is 1 eV equal to in joules? | 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ J is equal to what unit? | 1 electronvolt (1 eV)
How do you convert electronvolts to joules? | Multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
How do you convert joules to electronvolts? | Divide by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
What is the equation relating potential difference, energy, and charge? | V = E/Q
What happens when a charged particle is accelerated through a potential difference? | It gains kinetic energy
What is the equation relating electronvolt to kinetic energy for an electron accelerating from rest? | eV = ½mv²
What is the rearranged equation for the speed of an electron accelerated through a potential difference? | v = √(2eV/m)
What is the charge of an electron in coulombs? | 1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the photoelectric effect? | The phenomenon in which electrons are emitted from the surface of a metal upon the absorption of electromagnetic radiation
What are electrons removed from a metal by the photoelectric effect called? | Photoelectrons
What evidence does the photoelectric effect provide about the nature of light? | That light behaves as a particle (light is quantised/carried in discrete packets)
How many photons can a single electron absorb in the photoelectric effect? | Only one photon
What is the threshold frequency? | The minimum frequency of incident electromagnetic radiation required to remove a photoelectron from the surface of a metal
The minimum frequency of incident EM radiation required to remove a photoelectron from a metal surface is called what? | Threshold frequency
What is the threshold wavelength? | The longest wavelength of incident electromagnetic radiation that would remove a photoelectron from the surface of a metal
The longest wavelength of incident EM radiation that would remove a photoelectron from a metal surface is called what? | Threshold wavelength
What equation relates frequency and wavelength for waves? | v = fλ (or c = fλ for light)
For photons (particles of light), what does v equal in the wave equation? | c (the speed of light)
Are threshold frequency and wavelength the same for all metals? | No, they are properties of a material and vary from metal to metal
What is the work function (Φ)? | The minimum energy required to release a photoelectron from the surface of a metal
The minimum energy required to release a photoelectron from the surface of a metal is called what? | The work function (Φ) or threshold energy
In the energy well analogy, what does the depth of the well represent? | The work function (Φ)
Can an electron escape from a metal surface if the photon energy is less than the work function? | No, the electron cannot escape
Why do different metals have different work functions? | Because the attractive forces between surface electrons and positive metal ions differ between metals
In which region of the EM spectrum do alkali metals (sodium, potassium) have their threshold frequencies? | The visible light region
Why do alkali metals have threshold frequencies in the visible light region? | Because the attractive forces between surface electrons and positive metal ions are relatively weak
In which region of the EM spectrum do transition metals (zinc, iron) have their threshold frequencies? | The ultraviolet region
Why do transition metals have threshold frequencies in the ultraviolet region? | Because the attractive forces between surface electrons and positive metal ions are much stronger
What is stopping potential (Vₛ)? | The potential difference required to stop photoelectron emission from occurring
The potential difference required to stop photoelectron emission from occurring is called what? | Stopping potential (Vₛ)
In a photoelectric cell setup, what is the plate that emits photoelectrons called? | The emitter plate
In a photoelectric cell setup, what is the plate that collects photoelectrons called? | The collector plate
What is the photoelectric current? | The flow of photoelectrons across the gap between plates, creating an e.m.f. that causes current to flow
What happens to electrons leaving the emitter plate when the p.d. across the tube opposes their motion? | Electrons are attracted back towards the emitter plate
What is the equation for maximum kinetic energy using stopping potential? | E_k(max) = eVₛ
At stopping potential, what is the relationship between the energy needed to cross the gap and E_k(max)? | They are equal
What happens to the photoelectric current when intensity increases (at a given p.d.)? | The photoelectric current increases
What happens to the stopping potential when intensity increases? | The stopping potential remains the same
What does the constant stopping potential at different intensities demonstrate? | That intensity does not affect the kinetic energy of photoelectrons
What two factors determine the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons? | The frequency (or wavelength) of incident photons and the work function of the metal
If frequency increases while intensity remains constant, what happens to the photoelectric current? | It decreases (fewer photons per unit area per second)
Why does increasing frequency at constant intensity reduce photoelectric current? | Higher frequency means more energy per photon, so fewer photons are needed to maintain the same intensity
What is the equation for photon energy? | E = hf
What is the photoelectric equation? | hf = Φ + E_k(max)
Write the photoelectric equation including kinetic energy terms. | E = hf = Φ + ½mv²_max
In the photoelectric equation, what does h represent? | Planck's constant (J s)
In the photoelectric equation, what does f represent? | The frequency of the incident radiation (Hz)
In the photoelectric equation, what does Φ represent? | The work function of the material (J)
What is the condition for photoelectric emission to just occur? | hf₀ = Φ (where f₀ is the threshold frequency)
If incident photons do not have enough frequency/energy to overcome the work function, what happens? | No electrons will be emitted
Does E_k(max) depend on the intensity of radiation? | No, it depends only on the frequency of the incident photon
Do most photoelectrons have kinetic energy equal to E_k(max)? | No, the majority have kinetic energies less than E_k(max)
When graphing E_k(max) against frequency f, what does the y-intercept represent? | The negative work function (-Φ)
When graphing E_k(max) against frequency f, what does the x-intercept represent? | The threshold frequency (f₀)
When graphing E_k(max) against frequency f, what does the gradient represent? | Planck's constant (h)
What happens below the threshold frequency on an E_k(max) vs f graph? | No electrons are emitted
Why is the kinetic energy of photoelectrons independent of intensity? | Because each electron can only absorb one photon
What does increasing the number of photons striking a metal do? | Increases the number of photoelectrons emitted (not their kinetic energy)
Why do photoelectrons have a range of kinetic energies rather than all having E_k(max)? | Because some electrons are deeper in the metal and use energy reaching the surface
Which photoelectrons have the maximum kinetic energy? | Those on the surface of the metal (they require less energy to leave)
Why do deeper electrons have lower kinetic energy when emitted? | Some of the absorbed energy is used to reach the metal surface and overcome the work function
What does photoelectric current measure? | The number of photoelectrons emitted per second
How is photoelectric current calculated? | Number of electrons emitted multiplied by the charge on one electron
What is the relationship between photoelectric current and intensity? | Photoelectric current is proportional to the intensity of incident radiation
Why is photoelectric current proportional to intensity? | Because intensity is proportional to the number of photons striking the metal per second, and each photoelectron absorbs one photon
What happens to photoelectric current if frequency changes while the number of photons remains constant? | The photoelectric current remains constant
What is the equation for intensity in terms of photon energy? | I = nhf/tA (where n is number of photons)
If frequency increases at constant intensity, what must happen to the number of photons? | The number of photons must decrease
Why must the number of photons decrease when frequency increases at constant intensity? | Because at higher frequencies each photon has higher energy, so fewer are needed to maintain the same intensity
What units must hf, Φ, and E_k(max) have in the photoelectric equation? | The same units (joules)
How do you convert eV to joules for use in the photoelectric equation? | Multiply by 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹
What is the threshold frequency of sodium? | 4.40 × 10¹⁴ Hz
What is the threshold wavelength of sodium? | 682 nm
What is the threshold frequency of zinc? | 1.02 × 10¹⁵ Hz
What is the threshold wavelength of zinc? | 294 nm
What is the equation for photon energy in terms of wavelength? | E = hc/λ
What does the photoelectric effect demonstrate about energy transfer between photons and electrons? | Energy transfer is one-to-one (one photon transfers all its energy to one electron)
What is intensity equal to in terms of power and area? | I = P/A = E/tA
Why is the stopping voltage often quoted as just its magnitude in calculations? | Because although it has a negative value, we use it to determine maximum kinetic energy so the sign is not important
What is the coconut shy analogy for threshold frequency? | Table tennis balls (low frequency photons) won't knock off the coconut no matter how many are thrown, but one bullet (high frequency photon) knocks it off immediately
In the energy well analogy, what happens to an electron that absorbs a photon with energy equal to the work function? | The electron just reaches the top of the well and barely escapes with no kinetic energy
In the energy well analogy, what happens to an electron that absorbs a photon with energy greater than the work function? | The electron escapes and has kinetic energy equal to the excess energy above the work function
