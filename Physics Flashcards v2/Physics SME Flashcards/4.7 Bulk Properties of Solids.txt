What is density? | Mass per unit volume of an object
What is the equation for density? | ρ = m/V
What are the units of density when mass is in kg and volume is in m³? | kg/m³ (or kg m⁻³)
What are the units of density when mass is in g and volume is in cm³? | g/cm³ (or g cm⁻³)
Which state of matter is less dense: solids or gases? | Gases are less dense than solids
What is the formula for the volume of a sphere? | V = (4/3)πr³
What is the formula for the volume of a cube? | V = d³ (where d is the side length)
What is the formula for the volume of a cylinder? | V = πr²l (where r is radius and l is length)
When converting a larger unit to a smaller one, do you multiply or divide? | Multiply (×)
When converting a smaller unit to a larger one, do you multiply or divide? | Divide (÷)
How many m³ is 1 mm³? | 1 × 10⁻⁹ m³
How many m³ is 1 cm³? | 1 × 10⁻⁶ m³
What happens when a force is applied to each end of a spring or wire? | It stretches
What is elasticity? | The property of a material that allows it to return to its original shape after being stretched
What does Hooke's Law state? | The extension of a material is directly proportional to the applied force (load) up to the limit of proportionality
What is the equation for Hooke's Law? | F = kΔL
In Hooke's Law (F = kΔL), what does F represent? | Force (N)
In Hooke's Law (F = kΔL), what does k represent? | Spring constant (N m⁻¹)
In Hooke's Law (F = kΔL), what does ΔL represent? | Extension (m)
What does the spring constant measure? | The stiffness of a material
What does a larger spring constant indicate about a material? | The material is stiffer
Does Hooke's Law apply to both extensions and compressions? | Yes
What is extension? | The increase in length when an object is stretched
What is compression? | The decrease in length when an object is compressed
How do you calculate extension? | Extension = stretched length − unstretched length
On a force-extension graph, how is Hooke's Law region shown? | A straight line through the origin
What is the gradient of a force-extension graph equal to (in the Hooke's Law region)? | The spring constant (k)
What is the gradient of an extension-force graph equal to? | 1/k
What is the limit of proportionality? | The point beyond which Hooke's Law is no longer true and extension is no longer proportional to force
What is the elastic limit? | The point beyond which a material will no longer return to its original length when the load is removed
What is tensile stress? | The force exerted per unit cross-sectional area of a material
What is the equation for tensile stress? | σ = F/A
What are the units of tensile stress? | Pascals (Pa)
What is the symbol for tensile stress? | σ (sigma)
What is ultimate tensile stress? | The maximum force per original cross-sectional area a wire can support until it breaks
What is tensile strain? | The extension per unit length
What is the equation for tensile strain? | Strain = ΔL/L
Why is strain dimensionless? | Because it is the ratio of two lengths
How can strain be expressed as a percentage? | (extension ÷ original length) × 100
What is yield stress? | The force per unit area at which a material extends plastically for no/small increase in stress
What is breaking stress? | The maximum stress a material can stand before it fractures
What is the elastic region on a stress-strain graph? | The region up to the elastic limit where the material will return to its original shape when force is removed
What is the plastic region on a stress-strain graph? | The region after the elastic limit where the material is permanently deformed
What does the area under the Hooke's Law region of a stress-strain graph represent? | The elastic strain energy stored per unit volume
What is ultimate tensile stress (UTS)? | The maximum stress that a material can withstand
What is elastic strain energy? | The energy stored in a material when it is stretched within its elastic limit
What is the equation for elastic strain energy (when Hooke's Law applies)? | E = ½FΔL
Using Hooke's Law, what is an alternative equation for elastic strain energy? | E = ½k(ΔL)²
What does the area under a force-extension graph represent? | The work done (or elastic strain energy stored)
For a material obeying Hooke's Law, what shape is the area under the force-extension graph? | A right-angled triangle
What is a ductile material? | A material with a large plastic region that can extend/stretch into a new shape before breaking
Give an example of a ductile material. | Copper (or rubber)
What is a brittle material? | A material with very little or no plastic region that breaks with little deformation
Give examples of brittle materials. | Glass, concrete, ceramics
How does a brittle material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin with no or negligible curved region
How does a ductile material appear on a stress-strain graph? | A straight line through the origin, then curving towards the x-axis
What is elastic deformation? | Deformation where the object returns to its original shape when the load is removed
What is plastic deformation? | Permanent deformation where the object does not return to its original shape when the load is removed
When a metal wire is stretched beyond its limit of proportionality and then unloaded, what happens to the unloading line? | It is parallel to the loading line but does not pass through the origin
What does the area between loading and unloading lines represent for a plastically deformed wire? | The work done to permanently deform the wire
When loading and unloading a rubber band, which curve is higher? | The loading (stretching) curve
What does the area between loading and unloading curves for a rubber band represent? | The work done in heating the rubber (increase in thermal energy)
Why does a rubber band become warm when repeatedly stretched and contracted? | Some energy is transferred to heat energy
What three components make up vehicle suspension systems? | Tires, springs, and shock absorbers
What do shock absorbers do? | Absorb or dampen the compression and rebound of springs, converting kinetic energy into thermal energy
What happens if a wheel loses contact with the road surface? | It slams back down causing large vibrations and potentially damaging the vehicle
How do shock absorbers convert energy? | They convert kinetic energy from movement into thermal energy which is dissipated
What happens to shock absorber resistance when a vehicle hits a bump at higher velocity? | The resistance provided increases
What are the three types of energy involved when a vertical spring oscillates? | Elastic potential energy (EPE), kinetic energy (KE), and gravitational potential energy (GPE)
At maximum compression of a vertical spring, what is the kinetic energy? | Zero (the mass is stationary)
At the equilibrium position of an oscillating vertical spring, what is maximum? | Kinetic energy
At maximum extension of a vertical spring, what is maximum? | Elastic potential energy
For a horizontal mass-spring system, which energy type is not involved? | Gravitational potential energy
Mass per unit volume of an object is the definition of what? | Density
The stiffness of a material is measured by what property? | Spring constant
Force exerted per unit cross-sectional area is the definition of what? | Tensile stress
Extension per unit length is the definition of what? | Tensile strain
The maximum stress before fracture is the definition of what? | Breaking stress
Energy stored when a material is stretched within its elastic limit is called what? | Elastic strain energy
A material that breaks with little deformation and has no plastic region is called what? | Brittle
A material that can stretch significantly before breaking due to plastic deformation is called what? | Ductile
The point where extension is no longer proportional to force is called what? | Limit of proportionality
The point beyond which a material won't return to its original length is called what? | Elastic limit
