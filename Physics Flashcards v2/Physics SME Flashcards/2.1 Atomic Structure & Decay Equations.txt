What are the three types of particles that make up atoms? | Protons, neutrons, and electrons
Where are protons and neutrons found in an atom? | In the nucleus
Where are electrons found in an atom? | Orbiting the nucleus
What is the charge of a proton in SI units? | +1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the charge of a neutron? | 0 (no charge)
What is the charge of an electron in SI units? | −1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
What is the mass of a proton in SI units? | 1.67(3) × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the mass of a neutron in SI units? | 1.67(5) × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the mass of an electron in SI units? | 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg
What is the relative charge of a proton? | +1
What is the relative charge of a neutron? | 0
What is the relative charge of an electron? | −1
What is the relative mass of a proton? | 1
What is the relative mass of a neutron? | 1
What is the relative mass of an electron? | 1/2000 (negligible)
Why is a stable atom electrically neutral? | It has an equal number of protons and electrons, so their charges cancel out
What is specific charge? | The ratio of the total charge of a particle to its mass
What is the equation for specific charge? | Specific charge = Q/m (charge divided by mass)
What are the units of specific charge? | Coulombs per kilogram (C kg⁻¹)
What is the specific charge of an electron? | 1.76 × 10¹¹ C kg⁻¹
What is the specific charge of a proton? | 9.58 × 10⁷ C kg⁻¹
How do you calculate the specific charge of an ion? | Charge = electrons added/removed × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C); Mass = nucleons × (1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg); then divide charge by mass
How do you calculate the specific charge of a nucleus? | Charge = protons × (1.60 × 10⁻¹⁹ C); Mass = nucleons × (1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg); then divide charge by mass
If an ion gains electrons, is its specific charge positive or negative? | Negative
If an ion loses electrons, is its specific charge positive or negative? | Positive
What is the difference between an 'atom' and a 'nucleus'? | An atom consists of the nucleus and electrons; the nucleus contains only protons and neutrons
In ᴬ_Z X notation, what does A represent? | The nucleon number (mass number) - total number of protons and neutrons
In ᴬ_Z X notation, what does Z represent? | The proton number (atomic number) - total number of protons
What is nucleon number (A)? | The total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus
What is proton number (Z)? | The total number of protons in the nucleus
What defines an element? | A fixed number of protons in its atoms
What are isotopes? | Nuclei that have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons
What properties remain the same for isotopes of an element? | Proton number (Z) remains fixed
What properties differ between isotopes of an element? | Nucleon number (A) differs
Name the two isotopes of hydrogen | Deuterium and tritium
Why are some isotopes unstable? | They have an imbalance of neutrons and protons
What do unstable isotopes do? | Constantly decay and emit radiation to achieve a more stable form
What is isotopic data? | The relative amounts of different isotopes of an element present within a substance
How can the percentage abundance of different isotopes be obtained? | Using a mass spectrometer
What is a common use of isotopic data? | Carbon dating of archaeological artefacts
How does carbon dating work? | By comparing the ratio of stable carbon-12 to unstable carbon-14 in dead tissue versus living tissue
What two forces act between nucleons in a nucleus? | Repulsive electrostatic forces (between protons) and attractive gravitational forces
Why is gravitational force negligible in the nucleus? | Gravity is the weakest fundamental force, so it has negligible effect compared to electrostatic repulsion
What is the strong nuclear force? | An attractive force acting between all nucleons that is stronger than the electrostatic force and holds the nucleus together
What particles does the strong nuclear force act between? | Quarks (protons and neutrons are made of quarks)
At what separation is the strong nuclear force repulsive? | Below 0.5 fm (femtometres)
At what separation is the strong nuclear force attractive? | Between 0.5 fm and 3.0 fm
At what separation does the strong nuclear force have maximum attraction? | Around 1.0 fm (typical nucleon separation)
At what separation is the equilibrium position (resultant force = 0) for the strong force? | About 0.5 fm
What is the range of the strong nuclear force? | 0.5 to 3.0 fm (very small range)
How does the electrostatic force compare to the strong force at separations between 0.5 and 3.0 fm? | The strong force attraction is far more powerful than electrostatic repulsion
At what separation does the electrostatic force between protons become significant? | More than around 2.5 fm
Is the strong nuclear force influenced by charge? | No, it is the same strength between all types of nucleon (p-p, n-n, p-n)
What is the equilibrium position for protons where electrostatic and strong forces are equal? | Around 0.7 fm
What is 1 femtometre (fm) in metres? | 1 × 10⁻¹⁵ m
What happens to the strong nuclear force beyond 3 fm separation? | It becomes zero / has no effect
What are the three most common radioactive decay mechanisms? | Alpha decay, beta-minus decay, and beta-plus decay
What is an alpha particle? | 2 protons and 2 neutrons (equivalent to a helium nucleus)
In which type of nuclei is alpha decay common? | Large, unstable nuclei with too many nucleons
What happens to the proton number during alpha decay? | Decreases by 2
What happens to the nucleon number during alpha decay? | Decreases by 4
What is a beta-minus (β⁻) particle? | A high-energy electron emitted from the nucleus
What happens during beta-minus decay? | A neutron turns into a proton, emitting an electron and an anti-electron neutrino
What happens to the proton number during beta-minus decay? | Increases by 1
What happens to the nucleon number during beta-minus decay? | Remains the same
What is a beta-plus (β⁺) particle? | A high-energy positron emitted from the nucleus
What happens during beta-plus decay? | A proton turns into a neutron, emitting a positron and an electron neutrino
What happens to the proton number during beta-plus decay? | Decreases by 1
What happens to the nucleon number during beta-plus decay? | Remains the same
What is the parent nucleus? | The original unstable nucleus before decay
What is the daughter nucleus? | The new nucleus formed after radioactive decay
What is an electron neutrino? | A subatomic particle with no charge and negligible mass
What is the relationship between a neutrino and an anti-neutrino? | The anti-neutrino is the antiparticle of a neutrino
Which type of neutrino is produced during β⁻ decay? | Anti-electron neutrino
Which type of neutrino is produced during β⁺ decay? | Electron neutrino
Why was the existence of the neutrino hypothesised? | To account for the conservation of energy in beta decay
How do alpha particle energies appear on a graph? | As discrete spikes (specific energy values only)
How do beta particle energies appear on a graph? | As a continuous curve (range of energies)
Why do beta particles have a continuous range of energies? | The energy released is shared between the beta particles and neutrinos
What conservation principles apply to alpha and beta emission? | Conservation of momentum and energy
What is antimatter? | Particles identical to matter counterparts but with opposite charge
What is the antiparticle of an electron? | Positron
What is the antiparticle of a proton? | Antiproton
What is the antiparticle of a neutron? | Antineutron
What is the antiparticle of a neutrino? | Anti-neutrino
How is an antiparticle symbol typically written? | With a line above its corresponding matter particle symbol
What properties are the same between corresponding matter and antimatter particles? | Mass and rest mass-energy
What property is opposite between matter and antimatter particles? | Charge
What is rest mass-energy? | The energy equivalent to the mass of a particle when it is at rest
What is the mass of a proton/antiproton? | 1.67(3) × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the rest mass-energy of a proton/antiproton? | 938.257 MeV
What is the mass of a neutron/antineutron? | 1.67(5) × 10⁻²⁷ kg
What is the rest mass-energy of a neutron/antineutron? | 939.551 MeV
What is the mass of an electron/positron? | 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg
What is the rest mass-energy of an electron/positron? | 0.510999 MeV
What is a photon? | A massless "packet" or "quantum" of electromagnetic energy
What does it mean that photon energy is 'discrete'? | Energy is transferred as packets (quanta) rather than continuously
What is the equation for photon energy in terms of frequency? | E = hf
What is the equation for photon energy in terms of wavelength? | E = hc/λ
What do the symbols represent in E = hf? | E = energy (J), h = Planck's constant (J s), f = frequency (Hz)
What is the relationship between photon energy and frequency? | Higher frequency means higher photon energy (directly proportional)
What is the relationship between photon energy and wavelength? | Energy is inversely proportional to wavelength
What is annihilation? | The destruction of a particle-antiparticle pair when they collide and convert their mass into two gamma-ray photons
What is pair production? | The creation of a particle-antiparticle pair when a high-energy photon spontaneously converts its energy into mass
What are the two most common particle-antiparticle pairs that annihilate? | Proton-antiproton and electron-positron
Why are two photons produced in annihilation? | To conserve momentum (they move in opposite directions)
What is the minimum energy of one photon produced in annihilation? | Equal to the rest mass-energy of one of the particles (E_min = hf_min = E)
What is the minimum energy required for pair production? | Equal to the total rest mass-energy of both particles produced (E_min = hf_min = 2E)
Where does pair production typically occur? | When a photon with enough energy interacts with a nucleus
Why do the particle and antiparticle move in opposite directions after pair production? | To conserve momentum
What is conserved in both annihilation and pair production? | Mass and energy
How do you convert MeV to Joules? | 1 MeV = 1.60 × 10⁻¹³ J
