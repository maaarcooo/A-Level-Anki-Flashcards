What are energy levels? | Certain energy states that electrons in an atom occupy
Why do electrons occupy the lowest possible energy level? | Because this is the most stable configuration for the atom
What happens when an electron absorbs or emits a photon? | It can move between energy levels, or be removed from the atom completely
What is excitation? | When an electron absorbs enough energy to move up to a higher energy level
What is an excited state? | The state of an atom when an electron has moved to a higher energy level
What must an electron do to move to a higher energy level? | Absorb a photon
What is de-excitation? | When an electron moves back down to a lower energy level
What must an electron do to move to a lower energy level? | Emit a photon
What is ionisation? | When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged
What happens to an atom when an electron is removed? | The atom becomes ionised
What is the ionisation energy of an atom? | The minimum energy required to remove an electron from the ground state of an atom
What is fluorescence? | When an electron in an atomic orbital absorbs energy from an interaction with a photon or a collision with another electron
What are fluorescent tubes filled with? | Low-pressure mercury vapour with a phosphor coating on the glass
What happens when a high voltage is applied across a fluorescent tube? | Electrons flow from the cathode to the anode producing an electron beam
What do beam electrons do when they collide with mercury atoms? | They transfer kinetic energy to the atomic electrons in the mercury atoms
What happens to atomic electrons in mercury atoms after gaining energy? | They gain enough energy for ionisation to occur
Why do high energy electrons de-excite? | Because a high energy state is unstable
What type of photons are emitted when mercury electrons de-excite? | Photons in the UV range of wavelengths
What do UV photons do in a fluorescent tube? | They collide with electrons in the phosphor coating and excite them into a higher energy level
What type of photons are emitted when phosphor electrons de-excite? | Photons in the visible light range of wavelengths
How do phosphor electrons de-excite? | In stages, emitting photons at each stage
How can energy levels be represented? | As a series of horizontal lines
What does the line at the bottom of an energy level diagram represent? | The ground state (greatest negative energy)
What do the lines above the ground state represent? | Excited states (with decreasing energies)
What does the line at the top of an energy level diagram (0 V or infinity) represent? | The ionisation energy
When is a photon emitted? | When an electron moves from a higher energy state to a lower energy state
What determines the energy of an emitted photon? | The difference in energy between the energy levels in the transition
What are line spectra? | When excited atoms emit light of certain wavelengths which correspond to different colours
What are line spectra also called? | Line or atomic spectra
Why can elements be identified by their line spectrum? | Because each element produces a unique set of spectral lines and no two elements emit the same set
What are the two types of line spectra? | Emission spectra and absorption spectra
What causes a line in an emission spectrum? | An electron transitioning from a higher energy level to a lower energy level, emitting a photon
What does an emission spectrum look like? | A set of discrete wavelengths, represented by coloured lines on a black background
What equation relates photon energy to wavelength? | ΔE = hf = hc/λ
In the equation ΔE = hf = hc/λ, what does ΔE represent? | Change in energy level (J)
In the equation ΔE = hf = hc/λ, what does h represent? | Planck's constant (J s)
In the equation ΔE = hf = hc/λ, what does f represent? | Frequency of photon (Hz)
In the equation ΔE = hf = hc/λ, what does λ represent? | Wavelength of the photon (m)
What evidence do line spectra provide? | That electrons in atoms can only transition between discrete energy levels
How can an atom be raised to an excited state? | By the absorption of a photon
What is observed when white light passes through a cool, low pressure gas? | Light of certain wavelengths are missing
What is an absorption spectrum? | A continuous spectrum containing all the colours with dark lines at certain wavelengths
What do the dark lines in an absorption spectrum correspond to? | The differences in energy levels in an atom
Why do some wavelengths appear to be missing in an absorption spectrum? | When electrons return to lower levels, photons are emitted in all directions, rather than in the original direction
How do the wavelengths missing from an absorption spectrum relate to emission spectra? | They are the same as the corresponding emission spectra of the same element
What equation gives the energy of a photon from an energy level transition? | ΔE = hf = E₂ - E₁
In the photon energy equation, what does E₁ represent? | Energy of the higher level (J)
In the photon energy equation, what does E₂ represent? | Energy of the lower level (J)
What is the equation relating wavelength to energy level difference? | λ = hc/(E₂ - E₁)
What is the relationship between energy difference and wavelength? | The larger the energy difference, the shorter the wavelength (and vice versa)
What is wave-particle duality? | The phenomenon where light can behave as both a particle (photon) and a wave
How does light interact with matter such as electrons? | As a particle
What evidence supports light behaving as a particle? | The photoelectric effect
How does light propagate through space? | As a wave
What evidence supports light behaving as a wave? | Diffraction and interference of light in Young's Double-Slit Experiment
What is diffraction? | When waves pass through a gap or around an obstacle and the waves spread out
What did Einstein propose about light? | That light can be described as quanta of energy that behave as particles, called photons
What does the photon model explain about electromagnetic waves? | They carry energy in discrete packets called photons
According to the photon model, what equation quantises photon energy? | E = hf
In the photoelectric effect, how many photons can each electron absorb? | Only a single photon
Why does the wave theory of light not explain the photoelectric effect? | The wave theory does not support the idea of a threshold frequency
What does wave theory suggest about photoelectric emission? | Any frequency of light can give rise to photoelectric emission if exposure time is long enough
According to wave theory, how should the kinetic energy of emitted electrons change with intensity? | It should increase with radiation intensity
What actually happens when light intensity is increased above the threshold frequency? | More photoelectrons are emitted per second (not higher kinetic energy)
Who discovered that matter can behave as a wave? | Louis de Broglie
What evidence shows that electrons can behave as waves? | A diffraction pattern is produced when a beam of electrons is directed at a thin graphite film
Why is graphite film ideal for observing electron diffraction? | Because of its crystalline structure - the gaps between neighbouring planes of atoms act as slits
What does the electron diffraction pattern look like? | A series of concentric rings
What would the pattern look like if electrons acted only as particles? | The particles would be distributed uniformly across the screen (no pattern)
What happens to the diffraction ring diameter when accelerating voltage is increased? | The diameter of the rings decreases
What happens to the diffraction ring diameter when accelerating voltage is decreased? | The diameter of the rings increases
What equation relates the kinetic energy of electrons to accelerating voltage? | Eₖ = ½mv² = eV
What is the de Broglie equation? | λ = h/p
What does the de Broglie equation relate? | Wavelength (a property of waves) to momentum (a property of matter)
How can the de Broglie wavelength be written in terms of speed? | λ = h/mv
What is the de Broglie wavelength equation in terms of kinetic energy? | λ = h/√(2mE)
In the de Broglie equation, what does λ represent? | The de Broglie wavelength (m)
In the de Broglie equation, what does p represent? | Momentum of the particle (kg m s⁻¹)
In the de Broglie equation, what does m represent? | Mass of the particle (kg)
In the de Broglie equation, what does v represent? | Speed of the particle (m s⁻¹)
What is the relationship between momentum and de Broglie wavelength? | The greater the momentum, the smaller the de Broglie wavelength
What is the relationship between kinetic energy and momentum? | p = √(2mE)
What can be deduced from an observed electron diffraction pattern? | The structure of the crystal producing that pattern
Why can high energy electrons be used to look at the size of a nucleus? | Because they have a shorter wavelength
How does a smaller momentum affect the de Broglie wavelength? | It results in a longer wavelength
How does a larger momentum affect the de Broglie wavelength? | It results in a shorter wavelength
What happens to the wavelength when electron speed (kinetic energy) is increased? | The wavelength decreases
What happens to diffraction rings when electron kinetic energy is increased? | They appear closer together
What is the relationship between kinetic energy, momentum and wavelength? | Higher kinetic energy means higher momentum and shorter de Broglie wavelength
How does wavelength affect the radius of a diffraction pattern? | Longer wavelength produces a larger radius; shorter wavelength produces a smaller radius
What type of diffraction pattern do electrons with smaller momentum produce? | A more diffuse pattern (larger radius)
What is the diffraction grating equation? | d sin θ = nλ
What is quantum mechanics? | A relatively new field of research about the nature of matter at atomic scales
Who are considered the pioneers of Quantum Theory? | Max Planck, Niels Bohr and Albert Einstein
What is peer review? | A process where other scientists who are experts in a field evaluate a new theory
When is a new scientific theory validated? | When enough experimental evidence is found to support it
What must scientists check during peer review regarding validity? | Does the research achieve what it says it does? Is the method appropriate and how have errors been addressed?
What must scientists check during peer review regarding originality? | Are the results new or has similar research already been done? Has previous work been credited?
What must scientists check during peer review regarding significance? | Are the findings important or ground-breaking?
Why should claims from non-peer-reviewed research be questioned? | There could be problems with the method, accuracy of results, or conclusions drawn
What does repeatable mean in scientific research? | The same scientists can get the same results when repeating the experiment
What does reproducible mean in scientific research? | Other teams of scientists can get the same results
What is excitation? (reverse) | When an electron absorbs enough energy to move up to a higher energy level
What is ionisation? (reverse) | When an atom gains or loses an orbital electron and becomes charged
What is wave-particle duality? (reverse) | The phenomenon where light can behave as both a particle and a wave
What is diffraction? (reverse) | When waves pass through a gap or around an obstacle and the waves spread out
What is the de Broglie wavelength? (reverse) | The wavelength associated with a moving particle, given by λ = h/p
What is peer review? (reverse) | A process where other scientists evaluate new research for validity, originality and significance
What is an absorption spectrum? (reverse) | A continuous spectrum containing all colours with dark lines at certain wavelengths
What is an emission spectrum? (reverse) | A spectrum containing discrete wavelengths represented by coloured lines on a black background
What is the ground state? (reverse) | The lowest possible energy level an electron can occupy in an atom
What is an excited state? (reverse) | The state of an atom when an electron has absorbed energy and moved to a higher energy level
