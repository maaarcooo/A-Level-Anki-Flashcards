What causes electrical resistance in a metal wire? | Free electrons collide with metal ions as they flow through the wire
What happens when free electrons collide with ions in a metal wire? | They transfer some or all of their kinetic energy, causing electrical heating
What is resistance? | The opposition to the flow of charge caused by ions impeding the movement of free electrons
What three factors does resistance depend on? | Length of the wire, cross-sectional area, and resistivity of the material
What is the equation for resistance in terms of resistivity? | R = ρL/A (where R = resistance, ρ = resistivity, L = length, A = cross-sectional area)
How does resistance change when the length of a wire increases? | Resistance increases (directly proportional to length)
How does resistance change when the cross-sectional area of a wire increases? | Resistance decreases (inversely proportional to area)
What happens to resistance if the length of a wire is doubled? | The resistance doubles
What happens to resistance if the cross-sectional area of a wire is doubled? | The resistance halves
What is resistivity? | A property that describes the extent to which a material opposes the flow of electric current through it
What is the unit of resistivity? | Ω m (ohm metres)
Is resistivity a property of the material or the component? | Resistivity is a property of the material itself
What does resistivity depend on? | Temperature
How does resistivity relate to resistance? | The higher the resistivity of a material, the higher its resistance
Why is copper used for electrical wires? | Copper has relatively low resistivity at room temperature, so current flows through it easily
What is the approximate resistivity of copper at room temperature? | 1.7 × 10⁻⁸ Ω m
What is the approximate resistivity of aluminium at room temperature? | 2.6 × 10⁻⁸ Ω m
Why do insulators not conduct electricity? | They have such high resistivity that virtually no current flows through them
What is the approximate resistivity of glass? | 10¹² Ω m
What is the approximate resistivity of silicon at room temperature? | 2.3 × 10³ Ω m
How does cross-sectional area relate to diameter for a circular wire? | Area is proportional to diameter squared (A = πd²/4)
If the diameter of a wire doubles, what happens to its resistance? | Resistance drops to one quarter (area quadruples)
What happens to atoms in a solid when temperature increases? | They vibrate faster
Why does resistance increase in metals when temperature increases? | Faster vibrating atoms cause more collisions with electrons, impeding their flow
For a metallic conductor obeying Ohm's law, how does temperature affect resistance? | Increase in temperature causes increase in resistance; decrease in temperature causes decrease in resistance
What is a thermistor? | A non-ohmic conductor and sensory resistor whose resistance varies with temperature
What does NTC stand for in thermistors? | Negative Temperature Coefficient
How does resistance change in an NTC thermistor when temperature increases? | Resistance decreases
How does resistance change in an NTC thermistor when temperature decreases? | Resistance increases
What are some applications of thermistors? | Temperature sensors in ovens, fire alarms, and digital thermometers
In a series circuit with a thermistor, what happens to the p.d. across the thermistor when temperature decreases? | The p.d. increases (because resistance increases)
In a series circuit with a thermistor, what happens to the current when temperature decreases? | Current decreases (because total resistance increases)
What is a superconductor? | A material with no resistance below a critical temperature
What is the critical temperature (or transition temperature)? | The temperature at which a material becomes superconducting
What happens to electrical energy in a superconductor? | No energy is wasted as thermal energy because there is no resistance
What is the critical temperature of mercury? | 4.2 K
How can the resistivity of a material be lowered? | By lowering its temperature
What happens to resistivity when a material is cooled below its critical temperature? | Resistivity disappears entirely (becomes zero)
Why are superconductors useful? | They allow large electric currents to flow without energy loss
Name two main uses of superconductors. | Production of strong magnetic fields and reduction of energy loss in transmission of electric power
Give three applications that use superconductors. | MRI scanners, maglev trains, particle accelerators (also: transformers, generators, motors, fusion reactors, electromagnets, power cables, microchips)
How do maglev trains use superconductors? | Superconductors create extremely strong electromagnets needed to levitate the train
What speeds can maglev trains achieve? | Up to 603 km/h
What is the aim of the resistivity practical? | To determine the resistivity of a constantan wire
What is the independent variable in the resistivity practical? | Length of the wire
What is the dependent variable in the resistivity practical? | Current through the wire
What are the control variables in the resistivity practical? | Voltage through the wire and the material of the wire
What equipment is used to measure wire diameter in the resistivity practical? | Micrometer screw gauge
Why should diameter measurements be taken 5-10 times along the wire? | To calculate a mean diameter and reduce random errors
How is the ammeter connected in the resistivity practical? | In series with the wire
How is the voltmeter connected in the resistivity practical? | In parallel with the wire
Why should the current be switched off between readings in the resistivity practical? | To prevent the wire from heating up and changing its resistivity
How is resistance calculated in the resistivity practical? | R = V/I
What graph is plotted in the resistivity practical? | Length (L) on x-axis against Resistance (R) on y-axis
What does the gradient of the R against L graph represent? | ρ/A (resistivity divided by cross-sectional area)
How is resistivity calculated from the graph in the practical? | ρ = gradient × A (gradient multiplied by cross-sectional area)
What is the formula for cross-sectional area of a circular wire? | A = πd²/4 (where d is diameter)
What systematic error should be avoided in the resistivity practical? | The wire must start at 0 on the ruler to avoid zero error
Why should only small currents flow through the wire in the practical? | To keep temperature constant, as resistivity depends on temperature
What safety precaution should be taken with thin wires carrying high current? | Never touch the wire directly when the circuit is switched on; switch off if you smell burning
What causes resistance in materials? | Ions getting in the way of flowing electrons and causing collisions
What is the relationship between resistance and length? | Directly proportional (R ∝ L)
What is the relationship between resistance and cross-sectional area? | Inversely proportional (R ∝ 1/A)
What type of component is a thermistor? | A non-ohmic conductor
Which has higher resistivity: metals or semiconductors? | Semiconductors have higher resistivity than metals
Which has higher resistivity: semiconductors or insulators? | Insulators have higher resistivity than semiconductors
What is the resistivity of germanium at room temperature? | 0.6 Ω m
What is the resistivity of gold at room temperature? | 2.4 × 10⁻⁸ Ω m
How does the resistance vs temperature graph for a thermistor appear? | Curved line showing high resistance at low temperature, decreasing to low resistance at high temperature
On a resistivity vs temperature graph, what distinguishes a superconductor from a normal metal? | Superconductor's resistivity drops to zero at the critical temperature; normal metal's resistivity continues to decrease gradually
In which countries do maglev train systems currently exist? | Japan, South Korea, and China
What is the resolution of a micrometer screw gauge? | 0.01 mm
What is the resolution of a metre ruler? | 1 mm
How many readings should be taken in the resistivity practical? | 8-10 readings
What length intervals are typically used in the resistivity practical? | 0.25 m intervals (or 0.1 m intervals)
