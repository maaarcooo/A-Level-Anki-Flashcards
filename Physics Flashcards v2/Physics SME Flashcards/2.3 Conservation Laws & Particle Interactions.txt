What are the four fundamental interactions (forces)? | Gravity, Electromagnetism, Strong Nuclear (Strong Interaction), Weak Nuclear
Which fundamental force is the weakest? | Gravity
Which fundamental force is the strongest? | Strong Nuclear (Strong Interaction)
What is the range of the electromagnetic interaction? | Infinite
What is the range of the gravitational interaction? | Infinite
What is the range of the weak force? | Up to 10⁻¹⁸ m
What is the range of the strong force? | Approximately 10⁻¹⁵ m
Which particles does the gravitational interaction affect? | Particles with mass
Which particles does the electromagnetic interaction affect? | Particles with charge
Which particles does the weak interaction affect? | All particles
Which particles does the strong interaction affect? | Hadrons only
What are exchange particles? | Virtual particles that carry the fundamental force between interacting particles
What is another name for exchange particles? | Virtual particles or gauge bosons
Why are exchange particles needed in particle interactions? | Because there cannot be instantaneous action at a distance - one particle needs to "know" that the other is there
What is the exchange particle for the strong interaction (between nucleons)? | Pion (π⁺, π⁻, π⁰)
What is the exchange particle for the weak interaction? | W-boson (W⁺, W⁻) or Z-boson (Z⁰)
What is the exchange particle for the electromagnetic interaction? | Photon (γ)
What is the theorised exchange particle for the gravitational force? | Graviton (not yet discovered)
Why does gravity not play a part in particle interactions? | It is so weak that it only has a noticeable effect on large masses
What is the virtual photon? | The exchange particle that carries the electromagnetic force
What are the properties of the photon? | No mass, no charge, and it is its own antiparticle
What is the photon's antiparticle? | The photon is its own antiparticle
When do electromagnetic interactions occur? | Whenever two charged particles interact with each other
What is the electromagnetic force responsible for in atoms? | Binding electrons to atoms (due to attractive force between negative electrons and positive nucleus)
Why are hadrons subject to the strong interaction? | Because hadrons are made up of quarks
What is the exchange particle for the strong interaction between quarks? | The gluon
What is the exchange particle for the strong interaction between nucleons? | The pion
Why can't leptons interact with the strong force? | Because leptons are not made up of quarks
What is the weak interaction responsible for? | The radioactive decay of atoms
What are the exchange particles for the weak interaction? | W⁻, W⁺, or Z⁰ boson
What happens in β⁻ decay? | A neutron turns into a proton emitting an electron and an anti-electron neutrino
What is the equation for β⁻ decay? | n → p + e⁻ + v̄ₑ
Which exchange particle is involved in β⁻ decay? | W⁻ boson
What happens in β⁺ decay? | A proton turns into a neutron emitting a positron and an electron neutrino
What is the equation for β⁺ decay? | p → n + e⁺ + vₑ
Which exchange particle is involved in β⁺ decay? | W⁺ boson
What is electron capture? | When an atomic electron is absorbed by a proton in the nucleus resulting in the release of a neutron and an electron neutrino
What is the equation for electron capture? | p + e⁻ → n + vₑ
Which exchange particle mediates electron capture? | W⁺ boson
What happens in an electron-proton collision? | An electron collides with a proton, producing a neutron and an electron neutrino
Which exchange particle mediates an electron-proton collision? | W⁻ boson
How does the sign of the W boson relate to beta decay type? | W⁻ boson is exchanged in beta minus decay; W⁺ boson is exchanged in beta plus decay
What are Feynman diagrams? | Diagrams that represent particle interactions and decays, showing the exchange particles involved
What does the y-axis represent in a Feynman diagram? | Time
What does the x-axis represent in a Feynman diagram? | Space
What is a vertex in a Feynman diagram? | A point where particles and exchange particles meet, representing points of interaction
Where do incoming particles appear in a Feynman diagram? | At the bottom
Where do outgoing particles appear in a Feynman diagram? | At the top
How are particles represented in Feynman diagrams? | By straight lines with arrows
How are exchange particles represented in Feynman diagrams? | By wavy lines (without arrows on the line itself)
In which direction must arrows on particle lines point in Feynman diagrams? | Forward in time
What is the default direction of exchange particle transfer in Feynman diagrams? | From left to right (unless indicated by an arrow above the wavy line)
Where must hadrons/quarks be positioned in Feynman diagrams? | On the left side
Where must leptons be positioned in Feynman diagrams? | On the right side
Can hadrons and leptons meet at a vertex in Feynman diagrams? | No, they must never meet at a vertex
What must be conserved at each vertex in a Feynman diagram? | Charge, baryon number, and lepton number
Can lines cross over in Feynman diagrams? | No, lines must not cross over
In β⁻ decay, what does the neutron decay into? | A proton and a W⁻ boson
In β⁻ decay, what does the W⁻ boson decay into? | An electron and an anti-electron neutrino
In β⁺ decay, what does the proton decay into? | A neutron and a W⁺ boson
In β⁺ decay, what does the W⁺ boson decay into? | A positron and an electron neutrino
During electron capture, what does the proton decay into? | A neutron and a W⁺ boson
In β⁻ decay at the quark level, what transformation occurs? | A down quark turns into an up quark
In β⁺ decay at the quark level, what transformation occurs? | An up quark turns into a down quark
What does the W⁻ boson carry away in β⁻ decay? | The negative charge of the down quark (providing charge for electron and anti-neutrino)
What does the W⁺ boson carry away in β⁺ decay? | The positive charge of the up quark (providing charge for positron and neutrino)
What are the conservation laws that all particle interactions must obey? | Conservation of charge (Q), baryon number (B), lepton number (L), strangeness (S), energy (mass-energy), and momentum
What is special about strangeness conservation in weak interactions? | Strangeness does not need to be conserved in weak interactions - it can change by 0, +1, or -1
What values can quantum numbers (Q, B, L, S) take? | Discrete values such as 0, +1, -1, 1/2
How do you determine if a particle interaction can occur? | Check whether each quantum number is equal on both sides of the equation (except strangeness in weak interactions)
If baryon number is not conserved in an interaction, can the interaction occur? | No, the interaction is not permitted
If strangeness changes by +1 in a weak interaction, is this allowed? | Yes, strangeness can change by 0, +1, or -1 in weak interactions
What is the strangeness of a particle containing an s quark? | -1
What is the baryon number of a baryon? | +1
What is the lepton number of a baryon? | 0
What force allows two electrons approaching each other to experience repulsion? | Electromagnetic force
What is exchanged between two electrons to produce electrostatic repulsion? | A virtual photon
Why does β decay occur at the quark level? | Because of the weak interaction between quarks
In β⁻ decay, what is the quark composition change? | Neutron (udd) changes to proton (uud) - a down quark changes to an up quark
In β⁺ decay, what is the quark composition change? | Proton (uud) changes to neutron (udd) - an up quark changes to a down quark
What is the charge of a down quark? | -1/3
What is the charge of an up quark? | +2/3
What is the quark composition of a neutron? | udd (up, down, down)
What is the quark composition of a proton? | uud (up, up, down)
How are electrons and protons attracted to each other? | Via the electromagnetic interaction
When an electron and proton interact (collide), which force facilitates the collision? | The weak interaction
What equation is the same for both electron capture and electron-proton collisions? | p + e⁻ → n + vₑ
What is the difference between electron capture and electron-proton collision Feynman diagrams? | The direction/sign of the W boson (W⁺ for electron capture, W⁻ for electron-proton collision)
In electron capture, which particle acts on which? | The proton acts on the electron
In an electron-proton collision, which particle acts on which? | The electron acts on the proton
What common exam mistake occurs when drawing Feynman diagrams? | Missing out arrows indicating the direction of charged gauge bosons or particles
