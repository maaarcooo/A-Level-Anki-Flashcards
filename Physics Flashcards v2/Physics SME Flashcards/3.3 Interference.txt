What happens when two or more waves arrive at the same point and overlap? | Their amplitudes combine (superposition)
What is superposition? | When two or more waves overlap at a point and their amplitudes combine
State the principle of superposition | When two or more waves overlap at a point, the displacement at that point is equal to the sum of the displacements of the individual waves
What is interference? | The effect of superposition when waves overlap
What is destructive interference? | When two waves in antiphase combine and cancel each other out
What is antiphase? | When two waves are completely out of phase (180° or π radians apart)
What is constructive interference? | When two waves in phase combine and their resultant wave amplitude is larger
When does constructive interference occur? | When two waves are in phase (peaks align with peaks, troughs with troughs)
When does destructive interference occur? | When two waves are in antiphase (peaks align with troughs)
What happens to the resultant amplitude when two waves are neither in phase nor antiphase? | The resultant amplitude is somewhere between the two extremes
How are individual wave displacements combined during superposition? | They are combined in the same way as other vector quantities (positive and negative displacements add algebraically)
What is coherence? | When waves have the same frequency and a constant phase difference
What two conditions are required for waves to be coherent? | 1) Same frequency 2) Constant phase difference
Why must waves be coherent to produce interference patterns? | For waves to undergo constructive and destructive interference, they must be coherent
Give two examples of coherent light sources | Monochromatic laser light; Sound waves from two speakers emitting the same frequency
What is path difference? | The difference in distance travelled by two waves from their sources to the point where they meet
What is phase difference? | The distance between the phases (peaks and troughs) of coherent waves travelling parallel to each other at a point
How is path difference generally expressed? | In multiples of wavelength (λ)
What is the condition for constructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = nλ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
What is the condition for destructive interference in terms of path difference? | Path difference = (n + ½)λ (where n = 0, 1, 2, 3...)
What does n represent in interference equations? | The order of the maxima or minima (n = 0 is central maximum, n = 1 is first order, etc.)
What is a wavefront diagram? | A diagram where curved lines represent each wavefront (peak or trough) showing interference between waves
On a wavefront diagram, where does constructive interference occur? | Where peak meets peak or trough meets trough
On a wavefront diagram, where does destructive interference occur? | Where a peak meets a trough
Why are lasers ideal for analysing diffraction and interference patterns? | They produce light that is coherent (constant phase difference and frequency) and monochromatic (same wavelength)
What is monochromatic light? | Light of a single wavelength/frequency
What are bright fringes in a diffraction pattern? | Areas of constructive interference
What are dark fringes in a diffraction pattern? | Areas of destructive interference
Why do non-coherent light sources like filament bulbs produce white light? | Because they emit multiple wavelengths with no constant phase relationship
List four safety precautions when using lasers | 1) Never look directly at laser or reflection 2) Don't shine at people 3) Avoid reflections from shiny surfaces 4) Wear laser safety goggles
Why are lasers dangerous? | They produce a very high-energy beam that can cause permanent eye damage or blindness
How can two-source interference be demonstrated with sound? | Using two speakers emitting coherent sound of the same frequency
When does constructive interference occur with sound waves? | When compressions and rarefactions from each wave line up, making sound louder
When does destructive interference occur with sound waves? | When a compression from one wave meets a rarefaction from the other, causing zero sound
What technology uses destructive interference of sound? | Noise-cancelling headphones
How can two-source interference be demonstrated with microwaves? | Using a microwave transmitter with two slits and a moveable microwave detector
What does the detector measure in regions of constructive interference? | Maximum amplitude or intensity
What does the detector measure in regions of destructive interference? | Minimum or zero amplitude (no signal)
What is the relationship between intensity and amplitude of a wave? | Intensity is proportional to the square of the amplitude (I ∝ A²)
What is intensity of a wave? | Power per unit area (W m⁻²)
What is the relationship between energy transferred by a wave and amplitude? | Energy transferred is proportional to the square of the amplitude
What does Young's double-slit experiment demonstrate? | It produces both a diffraction and an interference pattern, proving light behaves as a wave
What are the two methods for producing interference in Young's experiment? | 1) Interference of two coherent wave sources 2) Single wave source passing through a double slit
What are maxima in an interference pattern? | Bright fringes formed by constructive interference of light
What are minima in an interference pattern? | Dark fringes formed by destructive interference of light
In Young's experiment, what determines whether constructive or destructive interference occurs? | The path difference between the two waves
What is the fringe spacing equation (double slit equation)? | w = λD/s
In the equation w = λD/s, what does w represent? | The distance between each fringe (fringe width/spacing) in metres
In the equation w = λD/s, what does λ represent? | The wavelength of the light source in metres
In the equation w = λD/s, what does D represent? | The distance between the slits and the screen in metres
In the equation w = λD/s, what does s represent? | The separation between the two slits in metres
How does fringe separation change if wavelength increases? | Fringe separation increases
How does fringe separation change if slit-screen distance D increases? | Fringe separation increases
How does fringe separation change if slit separation s decreases? | Fringe separation increases
What colour is the central maximum when white light passes through a double slit? | White (all wavelengths interfere constructively at this point)
In white light interference, which colour appears nearest the central maximum? | Violet/blue (shortest wavelength, diffracted least)
In white light interference, which colour appears furthest from the central maximum? | Red (longest wavelength, diffracted most)
What happens to the fringe patterns further from the central maximum with white light? | The spectra eventually merge without space between them as colours overlap
Who proposed that light was a stream of microscopic particles called corpuscles? | Isaac Newton (1672)
Why was Newton's corpuscle theory rejected? | It could not explain interference or diffraction effects
What did Christiaan Huygens propose in 1678? | The Wave Theory of Light to explain diffraction and refraction
What are Huygens' wavelets? | Every point on a wavefront is a source of waves that spread out and travel at the same speed as the source wave
What did Thomas Young's double-slit experiment prove (1801)? | That light is a wave that can undergo constructive and destructive interference
What did James Clerk Maxwell show in 1862? | That electric and magnetic fields obey the wave equation, meaning light is electromagnetic waves with E and B fields perpendicular to each other
What did Einstein discover about light in 1905? | That light behaves as a particle (demonstrated by the photoelectric effect)
What did Einstein call packets of light energy? | Photons
What is wave-particle duality? | The understanding that light behaves both like a wave and a particle
What is the independent variable in Young's double-slit experiment? | Distance between slits and screen (D)
What is the dependent variable in Young's double-slit experiment? | Fringe width (w)
What are the control variables in Young's double-slit experiment? | Wavelength of laser light (λ) and slit separation (s)
How should fringe width be measured to reduce uncertainty? | Measure across many fringes and divide by the number of fringe widths
What is the diffraction grating equation? | nλ = d sin θ
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does d represent? | The distance between adjacent slits (slit spacing) in metres
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does θ represent? | The angle between the normal and the maxima
In the equation nλ = d sin θ, what does n represent? | The order of the diffraction pattern
How is slit spacing d calculated from N slits per metre? | d = 1/N
What is the zero-order maximum? | The central beam where n = 0
How is the angle θ calculated from experimental measurements? | θ = tan⁻¹(h/D) where h is distance to maximum and D is grating-screen distance
Why should a set square be used in interference experiments? | To ensure the beam passes through the grating at normal incidence and meets the screen perpendicularly
How can systematic error be reduced when measuring fringe width? | Use a set square to avoid parallax error
How does using a grating with more lines per mm affect accuracy? | It produces greater values of h, which lowers percentage uncertainty
Why should interference experiments be conducted in a darkened room? | So the fringes are clear and easier to measure
What class should lasers be for school experiments? | Class 2 with maximum output no more than 1 mW
What is the typical wavelength of a standard school red laser? | 635 nm
Why is measuring across multiple fringes better than measuring one fringe? | It reduces the percentage uncertainty in the measurement
What causes the observed fringe intensities to be modulated in real double-slit experiments? | Each slit also diffracts light like a single slit, causing the central maximum to be brighter than outer fringes
What is the resultant amplitude when two waves of amplitude A interfere constructively? | 2A (double the individual amplitude)
What is the resultant amplitude when two waves of equal amplitude interfere destructively? | Zero (complete cancellation)

What does an abrupt change in phase between two waves indicate? | The waves are not coherent (inconsistent phase difference)
Why must path difference be a whole number of wavelengths for constructive interference? | So the waves arrive in phase with peaks aligned
Why must path difference be an odd number of half wavelengths for destructive interference? | So the waves arrive in antiphase with peaks aligned to troughs
